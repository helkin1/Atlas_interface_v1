# Factor 1: Experience Level
## How Training Experience Changes Optimal Programming

---

## 1. Volume Landmarks by Experience Level (MEV, MAV, MRV)

The volume landmark framework originates from Dr. Mike Israetel / Renaissance Periodization, built on dose-response research. The key shifts by experience level:

**Beginners:**
- **MEV:** ~4-6 sets/muscle/week. Untrained individuals respond to very low stimulus thresholds.
- **MAV:** ~6-10 sets/muscle/week. The dose-response curve is steep — small volumes produce large effects.
- **MRV:** ~12-16 sets/muscle/week. Beginners recover poorly from high volumes due to lower work capacity, so their MRV is lower in absolute terms, but they rarely need to approach it.

**Intermediates:**
- **MEV:** ~8-10 sets/muscle/week.
- **MAV:** ~10-16 sets/muscle/week.
- **MRV:** ~16-22 sets/muscle/week.

**Advanced:**
- **MEV:** ~10-14 sets/muscle/week. Growth requires a stronger stimulus due to attenuated anabolic signaling.
- **MAV:** ~14-22 sets/muscle/week.
- **MRV:** ~22-30+ sets/muscle/week. Only sustainable for 4-8 weeks before requiring a deload.

**Multiplier approximation (relative to intermediate baseline):**

| Level | MEV Multiplier | MAV Multiplier | MRV Multiplier |
|-------|---------------|---------------|---------------|
| Beginner | 0.5-0.6 | 0.6-0.65 | 0.7-0.75 |
| Intermediate | 1.0 | 1.0 | 1.0 |
| Advanced | 1.2-1.4 | 1.2-1.4 | 1.2-1.5 |

> **Note:** Advanced lifters' MEV is roughly 2-3x a beginner's MEV. Their MRV may be 1.5-2x a beginner's MRV.

**Critical gap:** Schoenfeld, Ogborn & Krieger (2017) noted a "paucity of research into very high training volumes (>10-12 weekly sets)" in their meta-analysis, meaning the upper end of these ranges is based more on practitioner consensus than controlled trials.

## 2. Beginner Volume Needs

**What the research shows:**

- **Krieger (2010):** 2-3 sets per exercise produced ~40% more hypertrophy than 1 set. However, no significant differences were found between 2-3 sets and 4-6 sets, suggesting diminishing returns begin early for untrained populations.

- **Schoenfeld et al. (2017):** Each additional weekly set was associated with a 0.37% increase in muscle size (ES increase of 0.023 per set, P = 0.002). The 10+ sets/week group showed significantly greater growth than the <5 sets group, but most subjects in the included studies were untrained or recreationally trained.

- **Barbalho et al. (2019):** 24-week study with trained men performing 5, 10, 15, or 20 sets/muscle/week. Found 5-10 sets produced outcomes equal to or better than 15-20 sets, with strength actually being *worse* in higher-volume groups for most exercises. This suggests an inverted U-shaped curve where excessive volume becomes counterproductive.

**The ceiling for beginners:** Going beyond ~12-14 sets/muscle/week is unlikely to help and may impair recovery. The ACSM position stand supports 1-3 sets per exercise, 2-3x/week, as sufficient for meaningful gains.

---

## 3. Advanced Volume Needs: Requirement vs. Tolerance

**Do advanced lifters genuinely need more volume?** Yes, and the evidence supports both a genuine *need* and an increased *tolerance*:

**Evidence for genuine need (anabolic resistance):**

- Trained individuals show attenuated muscle protein synthesis (MPS) responses to the same stimulus that produces robust responses in beginners. Consistent RT alters anabolic intracellular signaling, requiring a greater stimulus to trigger adaptation.

- Figueiredo (2019) linked ribosomal biogenesis to long-term muscle gains. Moderate-volume training produced greater ribosomal biogenesis responses than low-volume training, suggesting a mechanistic basis for why more volume drives more growth in trained lifters.

- Schoenfeld et al. (2019) studied resistance-trained men (1, 3, or 5 sets/exercise/session). Strength gains were similar across all groups, but hypertrophy was significantly greater in higher-volume conditions for elbow flexors, mid-thigh, and lateral thigh. This directly demonstrates that trained lifters need higher volumes specifically for hypertrophy (not strength).

**Evidence for tolerance as the enabling factor:**

- Pelland et al. (2025) found the posterior probability of volume positively affecting hypertrophy was 100%, with a 0.24% increase per additional set (95% CrI: 0.15-0.33). The relationship showed diminishing returns but no clear plateau up to ~31 fractional weekly sets.

**Bottom line:** Advanced lifters both *need* and *tolerate* more volume. The need is mechanistic (anabolic resistance), and the tolerance is what enables them to apply the higher stimulus without exceeding MRV.

## 4. Progression Rate by Experience Level

### Strength Progression Rates

| Level | Load Increase | Expected Rate |
|-------|--------------|---------------|
| **Beginner** | +2.5-5 kg/session (upper) / +5-10 kg/session (lower) | Hubal et al. (2005): 0-250% 1RM increase over 12 weeks (n=585). Typical: 30-40 kg squat gain in 6 weeks. |
| **Intermediate** | +2.5-5 kg every 1-3 weeks | Gains slow to weekly or bi-weekly increments. Primary limiter shifts from technique to recovery management. |
| **Advanced** | +1-2.5% per training block (4-8 weeks) | A 2.5 kg squat increase at 300 kg is a successful block. Annual gains of 1-5% on main lifts. |

### Muscle Mass Gains (Lyle McDonald model, broadly supported by literature)

| Year of Training | Expected Muscle Gain |
|------------------|---------------------|
| Year 1 | 9-11 kg (20-25 lbs) |
| Year 2 | 4.5-5.5 kg (10-12 lbs) |
| Year 3 | 2.3-2.7 kg (5-6 lbs) |
| Year 4+ | 1-2 kg (2-4 lbs) |

### Application to the Progression Engine

The current progression engine uses fixed weight increments (Barbells +5 lbs, Dumbbells +2.5 lbs). With experience-level awareness:
- **Beginners:** Current defaults are appropriate; can increase every session
- **Intermediates:** Progression should be evaluated weekly, not per-session; stall detection threshold should be longer (4-5 sessions)
- **Advanced:** Progression should be evaluated per training block (4-8 weeks); stall detection threshold should be 6-8+ sessions; periodized loading becomes important

## 5. Frequency by Experience Level

**Key meta-analyses:**

- **Schoenfeld & Krieger (2016):** Training 2x/week per muscle produced significantly greater hypertrophy than 1x/week (ES: 0.49 vs. 0.30, P = 0.002). Whether 3x/week is superior to 2x/week remained unclear.

- **Schoenfeld, Grgic & Krieger (2019 update):** When volume is equated, frequency does not significantly impact hypertrophy. Frequency matters primarily as a vehicle for distributing volume.

- **Pelland et al. (2025):** The posterior probability of frequency affecting hypertrophy was less than 100% (compatible with negligible effects). For strength, frequency had a 100% posterior probability of positive effect, with diminishing returns beyond ~3x/week.

**Practical summary:**
- **Beginners:** 2x/week per muscle is sufficient and well-supported.
- **Intermediates:** 2-3x/week — primarily to distribute higher volumes.
- **Advanced:** 3-4x/week — necessary to distribute 14-22+ sets/muscle without excessive per-session fatigue.

> **Note:** Only 2 of 10 studies in Schoenfeld (2016) used trained subjects, so the frequency-experience interaction is poorly characterized in meta-analytic data. The practical interaction is indirect: advanced lifters need more total volume, and spreading that across more sessions prevents excessive per-session fatigue.

## 6. Deload Frequency by Experience Level

**Research is sparse.** Most evidence comes from surveys and coaching practice, not RCTs.

**Survey data — Bell et al. (2024):** 246 competitive strength/physique athletes reported deloading every **5.6 ± 2.3 weeks** (range: 1-12 weeks) for a duration of **6.4 ± 1.7 days**. Primary reasons: decrease fatigue (92.3%), prepare for training block changes (64.6%), improve performance (59.8%).

**Experimental evidence — Schoenfeld et al. (2024):** A 1-week deload at the midpoint of a 9-week program *negatively* influenced lower-body strength but had no effect on hypertrophy, power, or endurance. This suggests deloads may not always be beneficial and timing matters.

**By experience level (practitioner consensus, limited direct evidence):**

| Level | Recommended Deload Frequency | Rationale |
|-------|------------------------------|-----------|
| **Beginner** | Every 6-8 weeks, or only when needed | Lower training loads = less accumulated fatigue. Many beginners can progress continuously for 8-12 weeks. |
| **Intermediate** | Every 4-6 weeks | Moderate loads and volumes create more systemic fatigue. Proactive deloads prevent performance stagnation. |
| **Advanced** | Every 3-5 weeks | High absolute loads and volumes near MRV accumulate fatigue rapidly. Most advanced programming builds deloads into every 4-week block. |

**Implementation:** Athletes reduce volume (fewer sets and reps) and intensity (lower load) while maintaining training frequency. Effort is reduced by increasing reps in reserve. 47.2% use pre-planned deloads, 13.4% purely autoregulated, 39.4% a combination.

## Critical Gap in the Literature

Most meta-analyses pool trained and untrained subjects or compare only two levels. True three-way comparisons (beginner vs. intermediate vs. advanced) with matched protocols and direct hypertrophy measurements are essentially nonexistent. The volume landmark ranges above are synthesized from dose-response meta-analyses, single-population studies, and expert practitioner frameworks — not from head-to-head experience-level comparisons. The Pelland et al. (2025) meta-regression is the strongest current evidence for dose-response curves, but it does not stratify by training experience in its primary analysis.
