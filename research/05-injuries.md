# Injury-Region Resistance Training Programming: Evidence-Based Guide

---

## 1. SHOULDER

### Affected Muscle Groups
- **Directly affected:** Rotator cuff (supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis), deltoids (all heads), upper trapezius
- **Indirectly affected:** Pectorals (bench press compromised), triceps (pressing limited), latissimus dorsi (pulling limited), biceps (anterior shoulder stress during curls)

### Volume Modifications
Shoulder pain affects up to 70% of individuals over a lifetime, with up to 50% experiencing persistent pain 6-12 months after seeking treatment. Research supports reducing volume on pressing and overhead movements rather than eliminating training entirely. Higher dosage for sets/repetitions and the application of resistance may be more beneficial for rotator cuff-related pain than low-load approaches, according to a systematic review using the FITT principle (Pieters et al., 2024, "The Efficacy of Exercise Therapy for Rotator Cuff-Related Shoulder Pain According to the FITT Principle," JOSPT). The key is redirecting volume toward scapular stabilization and rotator cuff strengthening while reducing volume on compound pressing.

### Exercise Modifications
**Avoid:**
- Behind-the-neck press and behind-the-neck pulldowns (forces extreme external rotation under load, compresses supraspinatus against acromion)
- Wide-grip bench press (increases acromioclavicular joint compressive forces)
- Upright rows with narrow grip (forces internal rotation with elevation — classic impingement position)
- Empty can exercise (Kolber et al., 2014 — increases superior humeral translation)
- Overhead pressing through full ROM when symptomatic

**Safe alternatives:**
- Close-grip bench press (significantly reduces AC joint compressive forces and rotator cuff demand) (Fees et al., 1998; Green & Comfort, 2007)
- Floor press or board press (limits humeral extension past the trunk, eliminating the impingement-risk portion of ROM)
- Neutral-grip dumbbell press (allows natural scapulohumeral rhythm)
- Landmine press (angled pressing path reduces overhead stress)
- Cable or dumbbell lateral raises (controlled, shorter lever arm)
- "Full can" exercise instead of "empty can" for supraspinatus activation (Reinold et al., 2004)
- Face pulls for posterior shoulder/scapular work

### Range of Motion Considerations
Board presses and floor presses are specifically recommended to limit humeral extension past the trunk. Pain between 70-120 degrees of shoulder elevation ("painful arc") is a red flag indicating impingement. Training should remain below or above this arc. Keeping elbows at approximately 45 degrees from the body during pressing reduces subacromial stress (Durall et al., 2001).

### Rehab/Prehab Volume
About 90% of shoulder impingement cases can be managed with physical therapy. Targeted rehabilitation emphasizing scapular positioning and humeral head control reduces subacromial pressure. Approximately 70% of patients experience pain relief from exercise-based treatment alone (Diercks et al., 2014, "Optimal Management of Shoulder Impingement Syndrome," PMC). Elastic resistance training (bands) over 4-12 weeks produces significant increases in isometric strength and performance outcomes (Colado et al., systematic review).

**Key rehab exercises:** Y-T-W series, band external rotations at 90 degrees, face pulls, serratus anterior wall slides, deep cervical flexor training, and scapular retraction holds.

### Return-to-Training Guidelines
Progressive reintroduction over 4-8 weeks. Begin with isometric holds, progress to concentric-only movements, then full ROM exercises. Increase load in small increments only when the desired repetitions can be performed with correct form and without pain.

## 2. KNEE

### Affected Muscle Groups
- **Directly affected:** Quadriceps (especially VMO), hamstrings, popliteus, gastrocnemius
- **Indirectly affected:** Glutes (squat/lunge patterns compromised), hip adductors, hip flexors, calves (reduced closed-chain loading)

### Volume Modifications
The OPTIKNEE consensus (Culvenor et al., 2022, "Rehabilitation After ACL and Meniscal Injuries: A Best-Evidence Synthesis," BJSM) found moderate-certainty evidence supporting neuromuscular electrical stimulation for quadriceps strength post-ACL, and similar effectiveness of open vs. closed kinetic chain exercises. Volume should be reduced on high-impact compound movements (squats, lunges) and redirected toward isolation work and neuromuscular control. Blood flow restriction (BFR) training at low loads (20-30% 1RM) shows promise for maintaining muscle mass during rehabilitation with reduced joint stress, though evidence on pain reduction is mixed.

### Exercise Modifications
**Avoid:**
- Deep squats past pain-free ROM (particularly with ACL/meniscal injuries)
- Leg extensions with full ROM (high anterior tibial shear force in terminal extension — problematic post-ACL)
- Plyometrics and high-impact jumping during acute phase
- Heavy loaded lunges with excessive forward knee travel

**Safe alternatives:**
- Terminal knee extensions (last 30 degrees only — safe for ACL, targets VMO)
- Wall sits / isometric squats at pain-free angle
- Leg press with limited ROM
- Step-ups (controlled eccentric)
- Nordic hamstring curls (strong evidence for ACL prevention — Myer et al., 2013)
- Hip-dominant movements: hip thrusts, RDLs (reduce knee joint loading while maintaining lower body training)
- Seated or prone hamstring curls
- Cycling (low impact, maintains aerobic capacity)

### Range of Motion Considerations
Open kinetic chain exercises (leg extensions) should be restricted to 90-40 degrees of flexion post-ACL to minimize anterior tibial translation. Closed kinetic chain exercises (squats) are generally safer across broader ROM ranges due to co-contraction of hamstrings and quadriceps. Progressive ROM expansion should be guided by pain and swelling response.

### Rehab/Prehab Volume
Neuromuscular training programs reduce overall knee injury risk in female athletes by 22%, and ACL injury risk by 50% (Arundale et al., 2018; Webster & Hewett, 2018, "Evidence-Based Best-Practice Guidelines for Preventing ACL Injuries in Young Female Athletes," PMC). Programs should include 2-3 sessions per week, >15 minutes per session, with landing stabilization, hamstring strengthening, and agility components. Compliance of 75% or greater is the most influential determinant of efficacy.

### Return-to-Training Guidelines
The clinical practice guidelines from the Academy of Orthopaedic Physical Therapy (Eckenrode et al., 2023, "Exercise-Based Knee and ACL Injury Prevention," JOSPT) recommend progressive loading with strength, neuromuscular, proprioceptive, agility, and plyometric exercises. Return to full training typically follows criteria-based milestones (e.g., limb symmetry index >90%, hop test performance) rather than fixed timelines.

## 3. LOWER BACK

### Affected Muscle Groups
- **Directly affected:** Erector spinae, multifidus, quadratus lumborum, transversus abdominis, internal/external obliques
- **Indirectly affected:** Glutes, hamstrings, hip flexors (all major compound movements affected — squats, deadlifts, rows, overhead pressing)

### Volume Modifications
Exercise therapy is the first-line treatment for chronic low back pain (CLBP) across most clinical guidelines internationally (George et al., 2021, "Interventions for Management of Acute and Chronic Low Back Pain: Revision 2021," JOSPT). Current recommendations are graded, individualized resistance or aerobic exercise at low-to-moderate intensity, 2 days per week for at least 6 weeks. Posterior chain resistance training (PCRT) focusing on thoracic, lumbar, and hip extensor musculature over 12-16 weeks shows highly favorable outcomes compared to general exercise (Tataryn et al., 2021, "Posterior-Chain Resistance Training vs. General Exercise for CLBP," PMC).

Volume on axial-loaded movements (squats, deadlifts, overhead press) should be significantly reduced during acute episodes. Training around the injury — maintaining upper body pressing, pulling, and unilateral lower body work — is preferred over complete cessation.

### Exercise Modifications
**Avoid during acute episodes:**
- Conventional deadlifts from the floor
- Heavy barbell squats
- Good mornings with heavy loads
- Seated overhead press (axial compression)
- Sit-ups and crunches (repeated spinal flexion under load — McGill, 2007)
- Bent-over barbell rows (sustained loaded flexion)

**Safe alternatives:**
- McGill Big 3: curl-up, side plank, bird dog (McGill, 2015, "Low Back Disorders: Evidence-Based Prevention and Rehabilitation")
- Hip hinge patterning with light load (kettlebell deadlifts, trap bar deadlifts)
- Belt squats (removes axial spinal load)
- Chest-supported rows (eliminates lumbar stabilization demand)
- Cable/machine rows with back support
- Single-leg exercises: Bulgarian split squats, step-ups (reduced spinal loading vs. bilateral)
- Pallof press, cable chops (anti-rotation core training)

### Range of Motion Considerations
Avoid end-range spinal flexion under load. Maintain neutral spine during all exercises. Partial ROM squats and deadlifts (rack pulls, elevated deadlifts) can allow continued training with reduced spinal stress. The literature supports motor control exercises (stabilization) for improving pain and function (Saini et al., 2025, "Evaluating the Impact of Cervical Stabilisation Exercises," also applicable to lumbar spine stabilization principles).

### Rehab/Prehab Volume
Coordination/stabilization exercise programs show the greatest benefit for CLBP (Owen et al., 2020, "Summarizing the Effects of Different Exercise Types in Chronic Low Back Pain," BMC Musculoskeletal Disorders). No single exercise type is definitively superior, but resistance training and motor control exercises consistently outperform aerobic-only or combined programs. Exercise progression based on specific criteria shows promising benefits in short-term pain reduction and function improvement (Almeida et al., 2025, "Therapeutic Exercise Progression in Patients with Nonspecific Low Back Pain," Journal of Pain Research).

### Return-to-Training Guidelines
Graded exposure: begin with bodyweight stabilization exercises, progress to loaded movements in neutral spine, then gradually reintroduce full compound movements. Increase load by no more than 10% per week. Monitor pain response over 24-48 hours post-training.

## 4. WRIST / HAND

### Affected Muscle Groups
- **Directly affected:** Wrist extensors (ECRB, ECRL, ECU), wrist flexors (FCR, FCU, palmaris longus), intrinsic hand muscles, forearm pronators/supinators
- **Indirectly affected:** All grip-dependent exercises compromised — deadlifts, rows, pull-ups, curls, pressing (grip on bar)

### Volume Modifications
Carpal tunnel syndrome affects approximately 3% of the population. Systematic review evidence (Page et al., 2012, updated 2024, "Exercise and Mobilisation Interventions for Carpal Tunnel Syndrome," Cochrane) found limited and very low quality evidence for exercise interventions, though nerve gliding exercises and splinting show the most reliable benefit. For resistance training, grip-intensive exercises should be reduced or modified with straps/wraps to offload the wrist while maintaining training volume for larger muscle groups. Avoid grip-squeezing exercises with CTS, as they increase carpal tunnel pressure.

### Exercise Modifications
**Avoid:**
- Heavy barbell wrist curls (excessive flexion loading)
- Exercises requiring forceful gripping when symptomatic
- Palm-down lifting (increases extensor strain — relevant for lateral epicondylitis crossover)

**Safe alternatives:**
- Lifting straps for deadlifts, rows, pull-ups (offloads wrist/hand)
- Neutral-grip handles (reduces wrist deviation)
- Fat grips paradoxically reduce peak grip force requirements for some individuals
- Open-hand exercises on machines where grip is not limiting
- Wrist extension exercises with light resistance bands (balances flexor-extensor ratio)
- Nerve gliding exercises (median nerve mobilization — gentle finger extension/flexion without wrist bending)

### Range of Motion Considerations
Wrist stability training combined with grip strengthening reduced pain from 4.23 to 1.87 on VAS (Kim et al., 2024, "Effects of Wrist Stability Training Combined with Grip Strength Exercise," PMC). Avoid end-range wrist flexion and extension under load. Splinting in slight extension may reduce symptoms during training.

### Rehab/Prehab Volume
Balanced flexor-extensor conditioning reduces mechanical load on the median nerve. A yoga-based approach (twice-weekly for 8 weeks) was shown to reduce pain and improve grip strength in CTS patients (Garfinkel et al., 1998, JAMA). Prevention exercise programs are effective for reducing hand/wrist pain symptoms (Madencioglu et al., 2024, "Effectiveness of Prevention Exercise Protocol Among Office Workers with CTS Symptoms," PMC).

### Return-to-Training Guidelines
Begin with wrist-unloaded exercises (machines, straps), progress to light grip work, then gradually increase grip-dependent loading. Monitor for numbness, tingling, and pain.

## 5. ELBOW

### Affected Muscle Groups
- **Directly affected:** Wrist extensors at ECRB origin (lateral epicondylitis), wrist flexors/pronator teres at medial epicondyle (medial epicondylitis), biceps, triceps, brachialis, brachioradialis
- **Indirectly affected:** All pulling movements (bicep curls, rows, pull-ups) and pushing movements (dips, pressing) compromised

### Volume Modifications
Lateral epicondylitis (tennis elbow) affects 1-3% of the adult population annually and is more common in the dominant arm (Bisset & Vicenzino, 2015, "Physiotherapy Management of Lateral Epicondylitis," Journal of Physiotherapy). Manual therapy and eccentric strengthening are the two treatments with greatest benefit.

### Exercise Modifications
**Avoid:**
- Reverse curls (direct stress on ECRB origin)
- Wrist extensions with heavy load
- Pull-ups with supinated grip (medial epicondylitis)
- Heavy tricep extensions with full elbow lockout
- Palm-down lifting

**Safe alternatives:**
- Heavy slow resistance (HSR) training: 3 sets of 15RM progressing to 3 sets of 6RM over 12 weeks — shown superior to conventional exercises for lateral epicondylitis (Raman et al., 2024, "Impact of Heavy Slow Resistance vs. Conventional Exercise in Lateral Epicondylitis," PMC)
- Eccentric wrist extension exercises (Tyler twist with Flexbar)
- Isometric holds at mid-range (though evidence is mixed — Rio et al. found pain increases rather than decreases with isometric exercise in lateral elbow tendinopathy specifically, unlike patellar tendinopathy) (Vuvan et al., 2023, "Isometric Exercise for Management of Lateral Elbow Tendinopathy," PMC)
- Exercises with forearm in pronated position and wrist elevated
- Scapular stabilization exercises (addressing proximal weakness that contributes to distal overload)

### Range of Motion Considerations
Exercises should be performed with the elbow extended, the forearm pronated, and the wrist elevated (Coombes et al., 2015). Counterforce bracing placed distal to the injury site can reduce tendon stress during training and is supported for improving pain pressure thresholds.

### Rehab/Prehab Volume
A comprehensive rehabilitation strategy should address both proximal scapular weakness and local elbow/wrist dysfunction along the kinetic chain (Coombes et al., 2019, "A Comprehensive Rehabilitation Program for Treating Lateral Elbow Tendinopathy," PMC). Rehabilitation should be multimodal and phased. Tendon adaptation is slower than muscle adaptation — regeneration times must account for this to prevent periods of excessive tendon strain.

### Return-to-Training Guidelines
Phased approach: isometric holds (pain-free) to slow eccentric loading to HSR to sport/training-specific movements. Expect 12-16 weeks for full recovery. Increase resistance in small increments only when pain-free performance is achieved.

## 6. HIP

### Affected Muscle Groups
- **Directly affected:** Hip flexors (iliopsoas, rectus femoris), gluteus maximus/medius/minimus, hip adductors, piriformis, hip external/internal rotators
- **Indirectly affected:** Quadriceps (squat compromised), hamstrings (deadlift compromised), lower back (compensatory loading), core stabilizers

### Volume Modifications
Conservative treatment for labral tears and FAI involves modifying activities for a short period and a targeted exercise regimen. Healing of the labrum takes approximately 8-12 weeks in ideal conditions. Reduce volume on deep squats, heavy deadlifts, and movements requiring combined hip flexion, adduction, and internal rotation. Maintain training volume on non-aggravating movements.

### Exercise Modifications
**Avoid:**
- Deep squats beyond pain-free depth (especially with FAI — combined flexion + internal rotation impinges labrum)
- Sumo deadlifts (extreme hip abduction + external rotation can stress labrum)
- Exercises with excessive anterior pelvic tilt (increases hip impingement)
- Adductor machine with heavy loads (if labral tear involves anterior-superior region)

**Safe alternatives:**
- Modified-depth squats with feet hip-width apart (Matache et al., 2025, "Management of Labral Tears in the Hip: A Consensus Statement")
- Hip thrusts (posterior chain loading without deep hip flexion)
- Reverse lunges (less anterior hip stress than forward lunges)
- Cable kickbacks, pull-throughs
- Side-lying hip abduction (targets gluteus medius without impingement positions)
- Single-leg RDL (Tier 1 exercise for all three gluteal muscles — Collings et al., 2023, "Gluteal Muscle Forces During Hip-Focused Injury Prevention Exercises," MSSE)

### Range of Motion Considerations
Avoid combined hip flexion + adduction + internal rotation (the FADIR position), which is the primary provocative test for labral tears and FAI. Modify squat depth to stay above the angle that provokes symptoms. Hip flexor stretching reduces stress on the labrum during movement (reduces anterior pelvic tilt).

### Rehab/Prehab Volume
**Tier 1 exercises for gluteus medius** (highest force production): bodyweight side plank (338-483 N), loaded single-leg squat (278-422 N), loaded single-leg RDL (283-405 N) (Collings et al., 2023).

**Tier 1 exercises for gluteus maximus**: loaded split squat (495-688 N), loaded single-leg RDL (500-655 N), loaded single-leg hip thrust (505-640 N).

The clam exercise records only low-to-moderate activity and is unlikely to elicit sufficient stimulus for strengthening — useful only in early rehabilitation for motor control (Macadam et al., 2020, "Systematic Review: Therapeutic Exercises for GMed and GMin Segments," PMC).

Peak gluteal forces increase by 28-150 N when adding 12RM external resistance vs. bodyweight alone.

### Return-to-Training Guidelines
Consensus statements recommend sport-specific training can begin after nonoperative management shows symptom resolution. Progress from isometric glute activation to loaded single-leg work to bilateral compound movements.

## 7. NECK

### Affected Muscle Groups
- **Directly affected:** Deep cervical flexors (longus colli, longus capitis), cervical extensors (semispinalis cervicis, splenius capitis), upper trapezius, levator scapulae, scalenes, SCM
- **Indirectly affected:** Deltoids (overhead work limited), upper back (heavy pulling may stress cervical spine), trapezius-dependent movements

### Volume Modifications
Exercise interventions probably reduce the risk for a new episode of neck pain by approximately 51% compared to no intervention (OR 0.49, 95% CI: 0.31-0.76) in the short term (Sareen et al., 2023, "Effectiveness of Exercise Interventions for Preventing Neck Pain," JOSPT). No single exercise type is definitively superior for chronic neck pain; resistance training and motor control exercises both show benefits but produce different neuromuscular adaptations (Gross et al., 2023, "Summarizing the Effects of Different Exercise Types in Chronic Neck Pain," BMC Musculoskeletal Disorders).

### Exercise Modifications
**Avoid:**
- Behind-the-neck press and pulldowns (cervical hyperextension under load)
- Heavy shrugs (excessive upper trapezius loading can exacerbate cervical pain)
- Barbell back squats (bar position creates cervical compression)
- Heavy deadlifts with cervical hyperextension
- Any exercise requiring sustained cervical extension

**Safe alternatives:**
- Safety squat bar or front squats (eliminates cervical compression from bar position)
- Machine-based chest/shoulder work (supported positions)
- Low-load deep cervical flexor training (specific motor control — Jull et al., 2008, "Effects of Deep Cervical Flexor Training," BMC Musculoskeletal Disorders)
- Cervical stabilization exercises targeting deep muscles and postural alignment (Saini et al., 2025, "Evaluating the Impact of Cervical Stabilisation Exercises," Musculoskeletal Care)
- Isometric neck strengthening in neutral positions

### Range of Motion Considerations
Avoid loaded cervical end-range positions. Maintain neutral cervical alignment during all exercises. Use mirrors or coaching cues to prevent cervical hyperextension during deadlifts and squats.

### Rehab/Prehab Volume
Deep cervical flexor (DCF) training is low-load, without external resistance, performed in supine. It improves posture, neuromuscular control, and reduces pain. DCF training improves sitting posture (Jull et al., 2008). Cervical stabilization exercises significantly reduce pain and improve functional capacity across 12 studies of moderate-to-high quality (Saini et al., 2025). Neck strengthening protocols can improve neck muscle strength, though evidence for injury prevention is currently limited to rugby union and American football populations (Farley et al., 2021, "Neck Strengthening for Concussion Prevention," PMC).

Recommended dosage: 2-3 sessions per week, progressing from motor control to loaded resistance over 6-12 weeks (Gross et al., 2015, "Effectiveness and Optimal Dosage of Exercise Training for Chronic Non-specific Neck Pain," PLOS One).

### Return-to-Training Guidelines
Begin with pain-free isometric holds, progress to dynamic movements through increasing ROM, then gradually reintroduce axial loading. Cervical symptoms should be fully resolved before returning to heavy compound movements with cervical loading.

## 8. ANKLE

### Affected Muscle Groups
- **Directly affected:** Peroneals (fibularis longus/brevis), tibialis anterior, tibialis posterior, gastrocnemius-soleus complex, intrinsic foot muscles
- **Indirectly affected:** Quadriceps, glutes, hamstrings (all standing/closed-chain exercises compromised), hip stabilizers (compensatory patterns)

### Volume Modifications
Athletes with a history of lateral ankle sprain have recurrence rates of 28-61%, and approximately 40% develop chronic ankle instability (CAI). Exercise-based rehabilitation reduces the risk of recurrent ankle sprain (RR 0.37, 95% CI 0.18-0.74 for acute sprains; RR 0.38, 95% CI 0.23-0.62 for functional instability) (Bleakley et al., 2022, "Exercise-Based Rehabilitation Reduces Reinjury Following Acute Lateral Ankle Sprain," PLOS One). Only 20% of rehabilitation exercises address muscle strength, and only 6.8% target eversion strength specifically, despite reduced eversion torque being a major risk factor for CAI (Wagemans et al., 2023, "Rehabilitation Strategies for Lateral Ankle Sprain Do Not Reflect Established Mechanisms of Re-injury").

### Exercise Modifications
**Avoid during acute phase:**
- All standing single-leg exercises
- Running, jumping, plyometrics
- Heavy calf raises through full ROM
- Lateral/cutting movements

**Safe alternatives:**
- Seated calf raises (reduces ankle instability demands)
- Leg press (foot stabilized on platform)
- Machine-based lower body work (leg extension, leg curl, hip abduction/adduction)
- Upper body training unaffected
- Progressive balance training: double-leg stance to single-leg to wobble board
- Resistance band eversion/inversion exercises (specifically targeting peroneal weakness)
- Proprioceptive training on unstable surfaces once pain-free

### Range of Motion Considerations
Regaining full ankle ROM is a primary rehabilitation goal. Dorsiflexion limitation is a predictor of future injury. Weight-bearing dorsiflexion stretches should be introduced early. Avoid forced inversion/eversion through end-range during acute phase.

### Rehab/Prehab Volume
Peroneal strengthening is essential — insufficient peroneal strength is associated with both CAI and recurrent injury (Mattacola & Dwyer, 2002, "Rehabilitation of the Ankle After Acute Sprain or Chronic Instability," PMC). Proprioceptive exercises (wobble board training) are effective for preventing recurrent sprains. Future interventions should incorporate open-chain joint position sense training, multiplanar single-limb challenges, and jumping/landing exercises that reflect actual injury mechanisms.

### Return-to-Training Guidelines
Progress from non-weight-bearing ROM exercises to partial weight-bearing to full weight-bearing. Introduce single-leg balance before dynamic movements. Criteria-based progression: pain-free walking, then jogging, then cutting/jumping. Full return typically 4-8 weeks for grade I-II sprains.

## 9. CHRONIC VS. ACUTE INJURY PROGRAMMING DIFFERENCES

### Acute Injuries
- **Initial phase:** Reduce or eliminate direct loading of the injured area. Training around the injury (maintaining volume on uninjured muscle groups) is strongly preferred over complete cessation (Kristensen & Franklyn-Miller, 2012, "Resistance Training in Musculoskeletal Rehabilitation," BJSM).
- **Mechanism matters:** Many acute muscle strain injuries occur during the eccentric phase of sudden, forceful muscle actions (Shaw & Maffulli, 2016). Eccentric exercises should be avoided initially, then reintroduced progressively.
- **Volume:** Near-elimination of direct volume on affected area for 1-4 weeks depending on severity. Maintain or even increase volume on unaffected areas to preserve training adaptations.
- **Intensity:** Very low (20-40% 1RM) if training the affected area at all, potentially with BFR to maintain muscle mass with reduced mechanical stress.

### Chronic Conditions
- **Do not eliminate training.** The evidence is clear that resistance training can increase muscle strength, reduce pain, and improve functional ability in patients with CLBP, knee osteoarthritis, and chronic tendinopathy (Kristensen & Franklyn-Miller, 2012).
- **Volume:** Moderate and consistent, typically 2 days per week minimum. RT guidelines effective in healthy populations can generally be applied in rehabilitation settings, though exact application of intensity/volume for maximal therapeutic effects remains unclear.
- **Intensity:** Low to moderate initially, progressed based on symptom response. A 10% increase in strength training volume reduces injury risk by more than 4 percentage points (Lauersen et al., 2018, "Strength Training as Superior, Dose-Dependent and Safe Prevention of Acute and Overuse Sports Injuries," BJSM).
- **Programming philosophy:** Graded exposure and progressive overload. The goal is building tissue capacity to tolerate load, not avoiding load. Autoregulated approaches (adjusting based on daily symptom levels) are preferred over rigid programming.

### Deloading for Injury Management
Athletes typically reduce volume (via fewer reps per set and fewer sets per week) while maintaining frequency during deloads. The main triggers for deloading include feeling "beat up" (muscle soreness, joint aches, pain — 62.6% of respondents) and performance stalls (54.1%) (Coleman et al., 2024, "Deloading Practices in Strength and Physique Sports," Sports Medicine - Open).

## Key Overarching Principles from the Literature

1. **Training through vs. around injuries:** The evidence consistently favors continuing to train unaffected muscle groups rather than complete rest. Maintaining training stimuli on non-injured areas preserves neuromuscular function and may accelerate recovery through systemic hormonal and circulatory effects.

2. **Dose-response for prevention:** Strength training is dose-dependent for injury prevention — each 10% increase in training volume reduces injury risk by >4 percentage points (Lauersen et al., 2018). Traditional strength training is the safest RT modality overall (Aasa et al., 2023, "Which Resistance Training is Safest to Practice," Journal of Orthopaedic Surgery and Research).

3. **Partial ROM is a valid tool:** When injury prevents full ROM training, partial ROM at lengthened muscle positions maintains hypertrophic stimulus (Pallares et al., 2021, "Effects of ROM on Resistance Training Adaptations," Scandinavian Journal of Medicine & Science in Sports; Pedrosa et al., 2021).

4. **Tendon vs. muscle adaptation rates:** Tendons adapt more slowly than muscle. For tendinopathy-related injuries (elbow, shoulder, Achilles), programming must account for slower tissue adaptation with adequate recovery between sessions and gradual load progression.

5. **Autoregulation over fixed protocols:** For both chronic conditions and return-from-injury, autoregulated approaches (adjusting volume and intensity based on daily symptom levels) consistently outperform rigid pre-planned programs.

## 1. SHOULDER

### Affected Muscle Groups
- **Directly affected:** Rotator cuff (supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis), deltoids (all heads), upper trapezius
- **Indirectly affected:** Pectorals (bench press compromised), triceps (pressing limited), latissimus dorsi (pulling limited), biceps (anterior shoulder stress during curls)

### Volume Modifications
Shoulder pain affects up to 70% of individuals over a lifetime, with up to 50% experiencing persistent pain 6-12 months after seeking treatment. Research supports reducing volume on pressing and overhead movements rather than eliminating training entirely. Higher dosage for sets/repetitions and the application of resistance may be more beneficial for rotator cuff-related pain than low-load approaches, according to a systematic review using the FITT principle (Pieters et al., 2024, "The Efficacy of Exercise Therapy for Rotator Cuff-Related Shoulder Pain According to the FITT Principle," JOSPT). The key is redirecting volume toward scapular stabilization and rotator cuff strengthening while reducing volume on compound pressing.

### Exercise Modifications
**Avoid:**
- Behind-the-neck press and behind-the-neck pulldowns (forces extreme external rotation under load, compresses supraspinatus against acromion)
- Wide-grip bench press (increases acromioclavicular joint compressive forces)
- Upright rows with narrow grip (forces internal rotation with elevation — classic impingement position)
- Empty can exercise (Kolber et al., 2014 — increases superior humeral translation)
- Overhead pressing through full ROM when symptomatic

**Safe alternatives:**
- Close-grip bench press (significantly reduces AC joint compressive forces and rotator cuff demand) (Fees et al., 1998; Green & Comfort, 2007)
- Floor press or board press (limits humeral extension past the trunk, eliminating the impingement-risk portion of ROM)
- Neutral-grip dumbbell press (allows natural scapulohumeral rhythm)
- Landmine press (angled pressing path reduces overhead stress)
- Cable or dumbbell lateral raises (controlled, shorter lever arm)
- "Full can" exercise instead of "empty can" for supraspinatus activation (Reinold et al., 2004)
- Face pulls for posterior shoulder/scapular work

### Range of Motion Considerations
Board presses and floor presses are specifically recommended to limit humeral extension past the trunk. Pain between 70-120 degrees of shoulder elevation ("painful arc") is a red flag indicating impingement. Training should remain below or above this arc. Keeping elbows at approximately 45 degrees from the body during pressing reduces subacromial stress (Durall et al., 2001).

### Rehab/Prehab Volume
About 90% of shoulder impingement cases can be managed with physical therapy. Targeted rehabilitation emphasizing scapular positioning and humeral head control reduces subacromial pressure. Approximately 70% of patients experience pain relief from exercise-based treatment alone (Diercks et al., 2014, "Optimal Management of Shoulder Impingement Syndrome," PMC). Elastic resistance training (bands) over 4-12 weeks produces significant increases in isometric strength and performance outcomes (Colado et al., systematic review).

**Key rehab exercises:** Y-T-W series, band external rotations at 90 degrees, face pulls, serratus anterior wall slides, deep cervical flexor training, and scapular retraction holds.

### Return-to-Training Guidelines
Progressive reintroduction over 4-8 weeks. Begin with isometric holds, progress to concentric-only movements, then full ROM exercises. Increase load in small increments only when the desired repetitions can be performed with correct form and without pain.

## 2. KNEE

### Affected Muscle Groups
- **Directly affected:** Quadriceps (especially VMO), hamstrings, popliteus, gastrocnemius
- **Indirectly affected:** Glutes (squat/lunge patterns compromised), hip adductors, hip flexors, calves (reduced closed-chain loading)

### Volume Modifications
The OPTIKNEE consensus (Culvenor et al., 2022, "Rehabilitation After ACL and Meniscal Injuries: A Best-Evidence Synthesis," BJSM) found moderate-certainty evidence supporting neuromuscular electrical stimulation for quadriceps strength post-ACL, and similar effectiveness of open vs. closed kinetic chain exercises. Volume should be reduced on high-impact compound movements (squats, lunges) and redirected toward isolation work and neuromuscular control. Blood flow restriction (BFR) training at low loads (20-30% 1RM) shows promise for maintaining muscle mass during rehabilitation with reduced joint stress, though evidence on pain reduction is mixed.

### Exercise Modifications
**Avoid:**
- Deep squats past pain-free ROM (particularly with ACL/meniscal injuries)
- Leg extensions with full ROM (high anterior tibial shear force in terminal extension — problematic post-ACL)
- Plyometrics and high-impact jumping during acute phase
- Heavy loaded lunges with excessive forward knee travel

**Safe alternatives:**
- Terminal knee extensions (last 30 degrees only — safe for ACL, targets VMO)
- Wall sits / isometric squats at pain-free angle
- Leg press with limited ROM
- Step-ups (controlled eccentric)
- Nordic hamstring curls (strong evidence for ACL prevention — Myer et al., 2013)
- Hip-dominant movements: hip thrusts, RDLs (reduce knee joint loading while maintaining lower body training)
- Seated or prone hamstring curls
- Cycling (low impact, maintains aerobic capacity)

### Range of Motion Considerations
Open kinetic chain exercises (leg extensions) should be restricted to 90-40 degrees of flexion post-ACL to minimize anterior tibial translation. Closed kinetic chain exercises (squats) are generally safer across broader ROM ranges due to co-contraction of hamstrings and quadriceps. Progressive ROM expansion should be guided by pain and swelling response.

### Rehab/Prehab Volume
Neuromuscular training programs reduce overall knee injury risk in female athletes by 22%, and ACL injury risk by 50% (Arundale et al., 2018; Webster & Hewett, 2018, "Evidence-Based Best-Practice Guidelines for Preventing ACL Injuries in Young Female Athletes," PMC). Programs should include 2-3 sessions per week, >15 minutes per session, with landing stabilization, hamstring strengthening, and agility components. Compliance of 75% or greater is the most influential determinant of efficacy.

### Return-to-Training Guidelines
The clinical practice guidelines from the Academy of Orthopaedic Physical Therapy (Eckenrode et al., 2023, "Exercise-Based Knee and ACL Injury Prevention," JOSPT) recommend progressive loading with strength, neuromuscular, proprioceptive, agility, and plyometric exercises. Return to full training typically follows criteria-based milestones (e.g., limb symmetry index >90%, hop test performance) rather than fixed timelines.

## 3. LOWER BACK

### Affected Muscle Groups
- **Directly affected:** Erector spinae, multifidus, quadratus lumborum, transversus abdominis, internal/external obliques
- **Indirectly affected:** Glutes, hamstrings, hip flexors (all major compound movements affected — squats, deadlifts, rows, overhead pressing)

### Volume Modifications
Exercise therapy is the first-line treatment for chronic low back pain (CLBP) across most clinical guidelines internationally (George et al., 2021, "Interventions for Management of Acute and Chronic Low Back Pain: Revision 2021," JOSPT). Current recommendations are graded, individualized resistance or aerobic exercise at low-to-moderate intensity, 2 days per week for at least 6 weeks. Posterior chain resistance training (PCRT) focusing on thoracic, lumbar, and hip extensor musculature over 12-16 weeks shows highly favorable outcomes compared to general exercise (Tataryn et al., 2021, "Posterior-Chain Resistance Training vs. General Exercise for CLBP," PMC).

Volume on axial-loaded movements (squats, deadlifts, overhead press) should be significantly reduced during acute episodes. Training around the injury — maintaining upper body pressing, pulling, and unilateral lower body work — is preferred over complete cessation.

### Exercise Modifications
**Avoid during acute episodes:**
- Conventional deadlifts from the floor
- Heavy barbell squats
- Good mornings with heavy loads
- Seated overhead press (axial compression)
- Sit-ups and crunches (repeated spinal flexion under load — McGill, 2007)
- Bent-over barbell rows (sustained loaded flexion)

**Safe alternatives:**
- McGill Big 3: curl-up, side plank, bird dog (McGill, 2015, "Low Back Disorders: Evidence-Based Prevention and Rehabilitation")
- Hip hinge patterning with light load (kettlebell deadlifts, trap bar deadlifts)
- Belt squats (removes axial spinal load)
- Chest-supported rows (eliminates lumbar stabilization demand)
- Cable/machine rows with back support
- Single-leg exercises: Bulgarian split squats, step-ups (reduced spinal loading vs. bilateral)
- Pallof press, cable chops (anti-rotation core training)

### Range of Motion Considerations
Avoid end-range spinal flexion under load. Maintain neutral spine during all exercises. Partial ROM squats and deadlifts (rack pulls, elevated deadlifts) can allow continued training with reduced spinal stress. The literature supports motor control exercises (stabilization) for improving pain and function (Saini et al., 2025, "Evaluating the Impact of Cervical Stabilisation Exercises," also applicable to lumbar spine stabilization principles).

### Rehab/Prehab Volume
Coordination/stabilization exercise programs show the greatest benefit for CLBP (Owen et al., 2020, "Summarizing the Effects of Different Exercise Types in Chronic Low Back Pain," BMC Musculoskeletal Disorders). No single exercise type is definitively superior, but resistance training and motor control exercises consistently outperform aerobic-only or combined programs. Exercise progression based on specific criteria shows promising benefits in short-term pain reduction and function improvement (Almeida et al., 2025, "Therapeutic Exercise Progression in Patients with Nonspecific Low Back Pain," Journal of Pain Research).

### Return-to-Training Guidelines
Graded exposure: begin with bodyweight stabilization exercises, progress to loaded movements in neutral spine, then gradually reintroduce full compound movements. Increase load by no more than 10% per week. Monitor pain response over 24-48 hours post-training.

## 4. WRIST / HAND

### Affected Muscle Groups
- **Directly affected:** Wrist extensors (ECRB, ECRL, ECU), wrist flexors (FCR, FCU, palmaris longus), intrinsic hand muscles, forearm pronators/supinators
- **Indirectly affected:** All grip-dependent exercises compromised — deadlifts, rows, pull-ups, curls, pressing (grip on bar)

### Volume Modifications
Carpal tunnel syndrome affects approximately 3% of the population. Systematic review evidence (Page et al., 2012, updated 2024, "Exercise and Mobilisation Interventions for Carpal Tunnel Syndrome," Cochrane) found limited and very low quality evidence for exercise interventions, though nerve gliding exercises and splinting show the most reliable benefit. For resistance training, grip-intensive exercises should be reduced or modified with straps/wraps to offload the wrist while maintaining training volume for larger muscle groups. Avoid grip-squeezing exercises with CTS, as they increase carpal tunnel pressure.

### Exercise Modifications
**Avoid:**
- Heavy barbell wrist curls (excessive flexion loading)
- Exercises requiring forceful gripping when symptomatic
- Palm-down lifting (increases extensor strain — relevant for lateral epicondylitis crossover)

**Safe alternatives:**
- Lifting straps for deadlifts, rows, pull-ups (offloads wrist/hand)
- Neutral-grip handles (reduces wrist deviation)
- Fat grips paradoxically reduce peak grip force requirements for some individuals
- Open-hand exercises on machines where grip is not limiting
- Wrist extension exercises with light resistance bands (balances flexor-extensor ratio)
- Nerve gliding exercises (median nerve mobilization — gentle finger extension/flexion without wrist bending)

### Range of Motion Considerations
Wrist stability training combined with grip strengthening reduced pain from 4.23 to 1.87 on VAS (Kim et al., 2024, "Effects of Wrist Stability Training Combined with Grip Strength Exercise," PMC). Avoid end-range wrist flexion and extension under load. Splinting in slight extension may reduce symptoms during training.

### Rehab/Prehab Volume
Balanced flexor-extensor conditioning reduces mechanical load on the median nerve. A yoga-based approach (twice-weekly for 8 weeks) was shown to reduce pain and improve grip strength in CTS patients (Garfinkel et al., 1998, JAMA). Prevention exercise programs are effective for reducing hand/wrist pain symptoms (Madencioglu et al., 2024, "Effectiveness of Prevention Exercise Protocol Among Office Workers with CTS Symptoms," PMC).

### Return-to-Training Guidelines
Begin with wrist-unloaded exercises (machines, straps), progress to light grip work, then gradually increase grip-dependent loading. Monitor for numbness, tingling, and pain.

## 5. ELBOW

### Affected Muscle Groups
- **Directly affected:** Wrist extensors at ECRB origin (lateral epicondylitis), wrist flexors/pronator teres at medial epicondyle (medial epicondylitis), biceps, triceps, brachialis, brachioradialis
- **Indirectly affected:** All pulling movements (bicep curls, rows, pull-ups) and pushing movements (dips, pressing) compromised

### Volume Modifications
Lateral epicondylitis (tennis elbow) affects 1-3% of the adult population annually and is more common in the dominant arm (Bisset & Vicenzino, 2015, "Physiotherapy Management of Lateral Epicondylitis," Journal of Physiotherapy). Manual therapy and eccentric strengthening are the two treatments with greatest benefit.

### Exercise Modifications
**Avoid:**
- Reverse curls (direct stress on ECRB origin)
- Wrist extensions with heavy load
- Pull-ups with supinated grip (medial epicondylitis)
- Heavy tricep extensions with full elbow lockout
- Palm-down lifting

**Safe alternatives:**
- Heavy slow resistance (HSR) training: 3 sets of 15RM progressing to 3 sets of 6RM over 12 weeks — shown superior to conventional exercises for lateral epicondylitis (Raman et al., 2024, "Impact of Heavy Slow Resistance vs. Conventional Exercise in Lateral Epicondylitis," PMC)
- Eccentric wrist extension exercises (Tyler twist with Flexbar)
- Isometric holds at mid-range (though evidence is mixed — Rio et al. found pain increases rather than decreases with isometric exercise in lateral elbow tendinopathy specifically, unlike patellar tendinopathy) (Vuvan et al., 2023, "Isometric Exercise for Management of Lateral Elbow Tendinopathy," PMC)
- Exercises with forearm in pronated position and wrist elevated
- Scapular stabilization exercises (addressing proximal weakness that contributes to distal overload)

### Range of Motion Considerations
Exercises should be performed with the elbow extended, the forearm pronated, and the wrist elevated (Coombes et al., 2015). Counterforce bracing placed distal to the injury site can reduce tendon stress during training and is supported for improving pain pressure thresholds.

### Rehab/Prehab Volume
A comprehensive rehabilitation strategy should address both proximal scapular weakness and local elbow/wrist dysfunction along the kinetic chain (Coombes et al., 2019, "A Comprehensive Rehabilitation Program for Treating Lateral Elbow Tendinopathy," PMC). Rehabilitation should be multimodal and phased. Tendon adaptation is slower than muscle adaptation — regeneration times must account for this to prevent periods of excessive tendon strain.

### Return-to-Training Guidelines
Phased approach: isometric holds (pain-free) to slow eccentric loading to HSR to sport/training-specific movements. Expect 12-16 weeks for full recovery. Increase resistance in small increments only when pain-free performance is achieved.

## 6. HIP

### Affected Muscle Groups
- **Directly affected:** Hip flexors (iliopsoas, rectus femoris), gluteus maximus/medius/minimus, hip adductors, piriformis, hip external/internal rotators
- **Indirectly affected:** Quadriceps (squat compromised), hamstrings (deadlift compromised), lower back (compensatory loading), core stabilizers

### Volume Modifications
Conservative treatment for labral tears and FAI involves modifying activities for a short period and a targeted exercise regimen. Healing of the labrum takes approximately 8-12 weeks in ideal conditions. Reduce volume on deep squats, heavy deadlifts, and movements requiring combined hip flexion, adduction, and internal rotation. Maintain training volume on non-aggravating movements.

### Exercise Modifications
**Avoid:**
- Deep squats beyond pain-free depth (especially with FAI — combined flexion + internal rotation impinges labrum)
- Sumo deadlifts (extreme hip abduction + external rotation can stress labrum)
- Exercises with excessive anterior pelvic tilt (increases hip impingement)
- Adductor machine with heavy loads (if labral tear involves anterior-superior region)

**Safe alternatives:**
- Modified-depth squats with feet hip-width apart (Matache et al., 2025, "Management of Labral Tears in the Hip: A Consensus Statement")
- Hip thrusts (posterior chain loading without deep hip flexion)
- Reverse lunges (less anterior hip stress than forward lunges)
- Cable kickbacks, pull-throughs
- Side-lying hip abduction (targets gluteus medius without impingement positions)
- Single-leg RDL (Tier 1 exercise for all three gluteal muscles — Collings et al., 2023, "Gluteal Muscle Forces During Hip-Focused Injury Prevention Exercises," MSSE)

### Range of Motion Considerations
Avoid combined hip flexion + adduction + internal rotation (the FADIR position), which is the primary provocative test for labral tears and FAI. Modify squat depth to stay above the angle that provokes symptoms. Hip flexor stretching reduces stress on the labrum during movement (reduces anterior pelvic tilt).

### Rehab/Prehab Volume
**Tier 1 exercises for gluteus medius** (highest force production): bodyweight side plank (338-483 N), loaded single-leg squat (278-422 N), loaded single-leg RDL (283-405 N) (Collings et al., 2023).

**Tier 1 exercises for gluteus maximus**: loaded split squat (495-688 N), loaded single-leg RDL (500-655 N), loaded single-leg hip thrust (505-640 N).

The clam exercise records only low-to-moderate activity and is unlikely to elicit sufficient stimulus for strengthening — useful only in early rehabilitation for motor control (Macadam et al., 2020, "Systematic Review: Therapeutic Exercises for GMed and GMin Segments," PMC).

Peak gluteal forces increase by 28-150 N when adding 12RM external resistance vs. bodyweight alone.

### Return-to-Training Guidelines
Consensus statements recommend sport-specific training can begin after nonoperative management shows symptom resolution. Progress from isometric glute activation to loaded single-leg work to bilateral compound movements.

## 7. NECK

### Affected Muscle Groups
- **Directly affected:** Deep cervical flexors (longus colli, longus capitis), cervical extensors (semispinalis cervicis, splenius capitis), upper trapezius, levator scapulae, scalenes, SCM
- **Indirectly affected:** Deltoids (overhead work limited), upper back (heavy pulling may stress cervical spine), trapezius-dependent movements

### Volume Modifications
Exercise interventions probably reduce the risk for a new episode of neck pain by approximately 51% compared to no intervention (OR 0.49, 95% CI: 0.31-0.76) in the short term (Sareen et al., 2023, "Effectiveness of Exercise Interventions for Preventing Neck Pain," JOSPT). No single exercise type is definitively superior for chronic neck pain; resistance training and motor control exercises both show benefits but produce different neuromuscular adaptations (Gross et al., 2023, "Summarizing the Effects of Different Exercise Types in Chronic Neck Pain," BMC Musculoskeletal Disorders).

### Exercise Modifications
**Avoid:**
- Behind-the-neck press and pulldowns (cervical hyperextension under load)
- Heavy shrugs (excessive upper trapezius loading can exacerbate cervical pain)
- Barbell back squats (bar position creates cervical compression)
- Heavy deadlifts with cervical hyperextension
- Any exercise requiring sustained cervical extension

**Safe alternatives:**
- Safety squat bar or front squats (eliminates cervical compression from bar position)
- Machine-based chest/shoulder work (supported positions)
- Low-load deep cervical flexor training (specific motor control — Jull et al., 2008, "Effects of Deep Cervical Flexor Training," BMC Musculoskeletal Disorders)
- Cervical stabilization exercises targeting deep muscles and postural alignment (Saini et al., 2025, "Evaluating the Impact of Cervical Stabilisation Exercises," Musculoskeletal Care)
- Isometric neck strengthening in neutral positions

### Range of Motion Considerations
Avoid loaded cervical end-range positions. Maintain neutral cervical alignment during all exercises. Use mirrors or coaching cues to prevent cervical hyperextension during deadlifts and squats.

### Rehab/Prehab Volume
Deep cervical flexor (DCF) training is low-load, without external resistance, performed in supine. It improves posture, neuromuscular control, and reduces pain. DCF training improves sitting posture (Jull et al., 2008). Cervical stabilization exercises significantly reduce pain and improve functional capacity across 12 studies of moderate-to-high quality (Saini et al., 2025). Neck strengthening protocols can improve neck muscle strength, though evidence for injury prevention is currently limited to rugby union and American football populations (Farley et al., 2021, "Neck Strengthening for Concussion Prevention," PMC).

Recommended dosage: 2-3 sessions per week, progressing from motor control to loaded resistance over 6-12 weeks (Gross et al., 2015, "Effectiveness and Optimal Dosage of Exercise Training for Chronic Non-specific Neck Pain," PLOS One).

### Return-to-Training Guidelines
Begin with pain-free isometric holds, progress to dynamic movements through increasing ROM, then gradually reintroduce axial loading. Cervical symptoms should be fully resolved before returning to heavy compound movements with cervical loading.

## 8. ANKLE

### Affected Muscle Groups
- **Directly affected:** Peroneals (fibularis longus/brevis), tibialis anterior, tibialis posterior, gastrocnemius-soleus complex, intrinsic foot muscles
- **Indirectly affected:** Quadriceps, glutes, hamstrings (all standing/closed-chain exercises compromised), hip stabilizers (compensatory patterns)

### Volume Modifications
Athletes with a history of lateral ankle sprain have recurrence rates of 28-61%, and approximately 40% develop chronic ankle instability (CAI). Exercise-based rehabilitation reduces the risk of recurrent ankle sprain (RR 0.37, 95% CI 0.18-0.74 for acute sprains; RR 0.38, 95% CI 0.23-0.62 for functional instability) (Bleakley et al., 2022, "Exercise-Based Rehabilitation Reduces Reinjury Following Acute Lateral Ankle Sprain," PLOS One). Only 20% of rehabilitation exercises address muscle strength, and only 6.8% target eversion strength specifically, despite reduced eversion torque being a major risk factor for CAI (Wagemans et al., 2023, "Rehabilitation Strategies for Lateral Ankle Sprain Do Not Reflect Established Mechanisms of Re-injury").

### Exercise Modifications
**Avoid during acute phase:**
- All standing single-leg exercises
- Running, jumping, plyometrics
- Heavy calf raises through full ROM
- Lateral/cutting movements

**Safe alternatives:**
- Seated calf raises (reduces ankle instability demands)
- Leg press (foot stabilized on platform)
- Machine-based lower body work (leg extension, leg curl, hip abduction/adduction)
- Upper body training unaffected
- Progressive balance training: double-leg stance to single-leg to wobble board
- Resistance band eversion/inversion exercises (specifically targeting peroneal weakness)
- Proprioceptive training on unstable surfaces once pain-free

### Range of Motion Considerations
Regaining full ankle ROM is a primary rehabilitation goal. Dorsiflexion limitation is a predictor of future injury. Weight-bearing dorsiflexion stretches should be introduced early. Avoid forced inversion/eversion through end-range during acute phase.

### Rehab/Prehab Volume
Peroneal strengthening is essential — insufficient peroneal strength is associated with both CAI and recurrent injury (Mattacola & Dwyer, 2002, "Rehabilitation of the Ankle After Acute Sprain or Chronic Instability," PMC). Proprioceptive exercises (wobble board training) are effective for preventing recurrent sprains. Future interventions should incorporate open-chain joint position sense training, multiplanar single-limb challenges, and jumping/landing exercises that reflect actual injury mechanisms.

### Return-to-Training Guidelines
Progress from non-weight-bearing ROM exercises to partial weight-bearing to full weight-bearing. Introduce single-leg balance before dynamic movements. Criteria-based progression: pain-free walking, then jogging, then cutting/jumping. Full return typically 4-8 weeks for grade I-II sprains.

## 9. CHRONIC VS. ACUTE INJURY PROGRAMMING DIFFERENCES

### Acute Injuries
- **Initial phase:** Reduce or eliminate direct loading of the injured area. Training around the injury (maintaining volume on uninjured muscle groups) is strongly preferred over complete cessation (Kristensen & Franklyn-Miller, 2012, "Resistance Training in Musculoskeletal Rehabilitation," BJSM).
- **Mechanism matters:** Many acute muscle strain injuries occur during the eccentric phase of sudden, forceful muscle actions (Shaw & Maffulli, 2016). Eccentric exercises should be avoided initially, then reintroduced progressively.
- **Volume:** Near-elimination of direct volume on affected area for 1-4 weeks depending on severity. Maintain or even increase volume on unaffected areas to preserve training adaptations.
- **Intensity:** Very low (20-40% 1RM) if training the affected area at all, potentially with BFR to maintain muscle mass with reduced mechanical stress.

### Chronic Conditions
- **Do not eliminate training.** The evidence is clear that resistance training can increase muscle strength, reduce pain, and improve functional ability in patients with CLBP, knee osteoarthritis, and chronic tendinopathy (Kristensen & Franklyn-Miller, 2012).
- **Volume:** Moderate and consistent, typically 2 days per week minimum. RT guidelines effective in healthy populations can generally be applied in rehabilitation settings, though exact application of intensity/volume for maximal therapeutic effects remains unclear.
- **Intensity:** Low to moderate initially, progressed based on symptom response. A 10% increase in strength training volume reduces injury risk by more than 4 percentage points (Lauersen et al., 2018, "Strength Training as Superior, Dose-Dependent and Safe Prevention of Acute and Overuse Sports Injuries," BJSM).
- **Programming philosophy:** Graded exposure and progressive overload. The goal is building tissue capacity to tolerate load, not avoiding load. Autoregulated approaches (adjusting based on daily symptom levels) are preferred over rigid programming.

### Deloading for Injury Management
Athletes typically reduce volume (via fewer reps per set and fewer sets per week) while maintaining frequency during deloads. The main triggers for deloading include feeling "beat up" (muscle soreness, joint aches, pain — 62.6% of respondents) and performance stalls (54.1%) (Coleman et al., 2024, "Deloading Practices in Strength and Physique Sports," Sports Medicine - Open).

## Key Overarching Principles from the Literature

1. **Training through vs. around injuries:** The evidence consistently favors continuing to train unaffected muscle groups rather than complete rest. Maintaining training stimuli on non-injured areas preserves neuromuscular function and may accelerate recovery through systemic hormonal and circulatory effects.

2. **Dose-response for prevention:** Strength training is dose-dependent for injury prevention — each 10% increase in training volume reduces injury risk by >4 percentage points (Lauersen et al., 2018). Traditional strength training is the safest RT modality overall (Aasa et al., 2023, "Which Resistance Training is Safest to Practice," Journal of Orthopaedic Surgery and Research).

3. **Partial ROM is a valid tool:** When injury prevents full ROM training, partial ROM at lengthened muscle positions maintains hypertrophic stimulus (Pallares et al., 2021, "Effects of ROM on Resistance Training Adaptations," Scandinavian Journal of Medicine & Science in Sports; Pedrosa et al., 2021).

4. **Tendon vs. muscle adaptation rates:** Tendons adapt more slowly than muscle. For tendinopathy-related injuries (elbow, shoulder, Achilles), programming must account for slower tissue adaptation with adequate recovery between sessions and gradual load progression.

5. **Autoregulation over fixed protocols:** For both chronic conditions and return-from-injury, autoregulated approaches (adjusting volume and intensity based on daily symptom levels) consistently outperform rigid pre-planned programs.
