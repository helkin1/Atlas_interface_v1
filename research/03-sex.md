# Factor 3: Biological Sex
## Sex-Based Differences in Resistance Training Programming

---

## 1. Recovery Between Sets and Sessions

**The finding that women recover faster between sets is robust and has a meaningful magnitude.**

The most direct evidence comes from Nuckols et al. (2026), published in *PeerJ*. In this study, 21 males and 21 females with at least one year of bench press experience performed sets of 5 reps at 75% 1RM with 90-second rest periods until failure. Despite both sexes completing a similar number of reps on a single set to failure, **females completed nearly twice as many total sets** during the fatigue protocol. The primary driver was enhanced between-set recovery, not reduced within-set fatigue.

Critically, **post-exercise lactate was substantially lower in females** despite completing roughly double the volume, suggesting less reliance on anaerobic glycolysis and more oxidative metabolism. This aligns with the metabolic hypothesis: women preferentially use oxidative pathways, while men rely more on glycolytic pathways.

**However, recovery between sessions was similar.** In the same Nuckols et al. study, soreness ratings and estimated 1RM recovery over 72 hours did not differ between sexes. This is an important distinction: faster intra-session recovery does not automatically mean faster inter-session recovery.

The broader evidence base supports this pattern. Hunter (2014) in *Acta Physiologica* established that women are generally less fatigable during isometric and slow dynamic contractions at the same relative intensity. Proposed mechanisms include:
- Smaller muscle cross-sectional area leading to less arterial occlusion during contractions
- Greater muscle capillarization and blood flow
- Higher proportion of fatigue-resistant Type I fibers
- Possible protective effects of estrogen

A scoping review by Amdi et al. (2025) in the *International Journal of Sports Medicine* confirmed these trends but noted substantial heterogeneity and that it remains unclear whether these differences persist fully in well-trained populations.

**Confidence level:** High for between-set recovery differences. Moderate for between-session recovery (fewer direct comparisons; the Nuckols 2026 data suggest similarity).

## 2. Volume Tolerance (MRV Differences)

**Women likely have higher relative MRV, primarily due to superior between-set fatigue resistance.**

The physiological basis is straightforward: if women fatigue less per set and recover faster between sets, they can tolerate more sets per session before exceeding recovery capacity. Estimates from applied sport science sources suggest women can handle approximately **20-30% more training volume** than men, though this figure is derived more from practical observation than from controlled dose-response studies.

The Roberts, Nuckols & Krieger (2020) meta-analysis in *JSCR* is the most rigorous comparison: when men and women followed the **same training protocol** (same volume), hypertrophy outcomes did not differ (ES = 0.07, P = 0.31). This means that at matched volumes, women are not under-stimulated — but it also means the studies did not test whether women would have responded better to higher volumes.

No controlled study has directly compared dose-response curves for volume between sexes (e.g., giving women progressively more sets and measuring when MRV is reached vs. men). The claim that women have higher MRV is a mechanistically plausible inference from fatigue resistance data, not a directly measured outcome.

**Confidence level:** Moderate. The mechanistic reasoning is sound, but direct volume dose-response comparisons by sex are absent from the literature.

### Application to the Personalization Engine

Given moderate confidence, a conservative MRV modifier of **1.05-1.10** for women is appropriate. This is small enough to be safe (won't push anyone into overtraining) but directionally correct per the evidence.

## 3. Frequency Tolerance

**Women may benefit more from higher training frequency, but the evidence is confounded by volume.**

A meta-analysis by Grgic et al. (2018) in *Sports Medicine* found that higher resistance training frequency had a **significant effect on strength gains in women (P = 0.030) but not in men (P = 0.190)**. However, when volume was equated, the frequency effect disappeared for both sexes. This suggests that the frequency advantage for women may be a proxy for their ability to accumulate more volume across the week rather than a true frequency-specific effect.

The general frequency literature (Schoenfeld et al., 2016) shows a trivial advantage for higher frequencies when volume is matched (d = 0.113, P < 0.0001), with no sex-specific subgroup analysis.

**Confidence level:** Low to moderate. Likely volume-confounded. The practical recommendation — that women can train muscle groups more frequently because they recover faster between sets — is mechanistically reasonable but not directly validated.

## 4. Relative Strength Distribution (Upper vs. Lower Body)

**The sex difference in strength is substantially larger in the upper body than the lower body.**

Miller et al. (1993) in *European Journal of Applied Physiology* found women were approximately **52% as strong as men in the upper body** and **66% as strong in the lower body**. This is one of the most replicated findings in exercise science.

Key data points:
- Women have 45% smaller biceps brachii CSA but only 25% smaller knee extensor CSA (Miller et al., 1993)
- When normalized to lean body mass, the gap narrows considerably, especially in the lower body
- Force per unit of muscle cross-sectional area does not differ between sexes — the difference is entirely muscle mass-driven
- The upper/lower disparity emerges at puberty and is driven by androgenic effects on upper body musculature

**Training adaptation implications from Roberts et al. (2020):**
- Hypertrophy: No sex difference (ES = 0.07, P = 0.31)
- Upper-body strength gains: **Significant effect favoring females** (ES = -0.60, P = 0.002), meaning untrained women show greater relative upper-body strength gains
- Lower-body strength gains: No sex difference (ES = -0.21, P = 0.20)

**Programming implication:** Women start with a larger upper-body deficit. The finding that women make relatively greater upper-body strength gains suggests the upper body is more "trainable" from a low baseline. Women who want balanced development may benefit from proportionally more upper-body training volume, not because they respond differently, but because they have more room to grow.

**Confidence level:** High. Among the most well-established findings in the field.

## 5. Hormonal Cycle Effects

**The evidence does not support meaningful menstrual cycle effects on resistance training performance or adaptations. "Cycle-based training" is not well-supported.**

The most comprehensive assessment is the umbrella review by Colenso-Semple et al. (2023) in *Frontiers in Sports and Active Living*, which reviewed all existing meta-analyses and systematic reviews. Their conclusion: **"It is premature to conclude that short-term fluctuations in reproductive hormones appreciably influence acute exercise performance or longer-term strength or hypertrophic adaptations."**

Key findings across the literature:

- **McNulty et al. (2020)** meta-analysis in *Sports Medicine*: Exercise performance may be "trivially reduced" during the early follicular phase compared to other phases, but the effect size was trivial, between-study variation was large, and study quality was generally poor.

- **Blagrove et al. (2020)** in *Journal of Science and Medicine in Sport*: 21 studies, 232 participants — high risk of bias in most studies. No consistent pattern.

- **A 2024 meta-analysis** in *Sports (MDPI)*: Found medium effects for isometric strength favoring the late follicular phase (SMD = 0.60), but 68% of studies achieved only low or very low quality scores.

**On "cycle-based training" specifically:** The most-cited study (Wikstrom-Frisen et al., 2017) found follicular-phase-based high-frequency training produced greater strength gains than luteal-phase-based training. However, this study has been criticized for assuming 28-day cycles with mid-cycle ovulation in all participants, using only basal body temperature for phase verification, and essentially implementing biweekly undulating periodization mislabeled as "menstrual cycle-based training."

**Bottom line:** Individual women may notice meaningful cycle-related performance fluctuations, and acknowledging this is valid. But the evidence does not support systematic cycle-based periodization as superior to standard periodization.

### Application to the Personalization Engine

Menstrual cycle-based adjustments should NOT be implemented. The evidence is insufficient to justify building this into the system, and overcorrecting here could be worse than no correction.

**Confidence level:** Moderate-to-high that cycle effects are trivial at the population level.

## 6. Muscle Fiber Type Differences

**Women have a greater proportion of Type I fibers and men have a greater proportion of Type II fibers. The differences are statistically significant but modest in magnitude.**

**Nuzzo (2024)** in *Clinical Anatomy* (2,875 men, 2,452 women, 110 studies):
- Men: greater Type II distribution (g = 0.26-0.34) and Type II area percentages (g = 0.39-0.93)
- Women: greater Type I distribution (g = -0.13 to -0.44) and Type I area percentages (g = -0.53 to -0.69)
- Women had a much greater Type I/II fiber area ratio (g = -1.24)

**Sundberg et al. (2025)** in a systematic review with meta-analysis (6,222 participants, 156 studies):
- Males: Type II distribution 51.6 ± 14.6% vs. females 48.3 ± 13.0%
- Females: Type I distribution 51.4 ± 12.1% vs. males 48.3 ± 13.3%
- These differences persisted regardless of age, physical activity level, muscle group, and analysis technique

**Rep range implications:** The higher proportion of Type I fibers in women provides a mechanistic basis for their fatigue resistance but does NOT necessarily mean women should train at higher rep ranges. Type II fibers are the primary drivers of hypertrophy in both sexes, and women's Type II fibers hypertrophy at the same relative rate as men's (Refalo et al., 2025). The fiber type difference explains WHY women fatigue less (supporting higher volume), not that they need different rep ranges.

**Confidence level:** High for the existence and direction of fiber type differences. Low for any specific rep range recommendations based solely on fiber type distribution.

## 7. Hypertrophy Response

**Women gain muscle at the same relative rate as men. Absolute gains are slightly larger in men.**

**Refalo et al. (2025)**, a Bayesian meta-analysis in *PeerJ* (29 studies, 1,278 males, 1,537 females):
- **Absolute hypertrophy:** Slightly favored males (SMD = 0.19)
- **Relative hypertrophy (% change from baseline):** Virtually identical (0.69% difference between sexes)
- **Type I fiber hypertrophy:** Favored males (SMD = 0.39 absolute, 12.7% relative), with >90% probability
- **Type II fiber hypertrophy:** Similar between sexes (-2.29% relative change, slightly favoring females)
- Training status (trained vs. untrained) did not moderate the sex difference

The earlier **Roberts, Nuckols & Krieger (2020)** meta-analysis found no significant sex difference in hypertrophy (ES = 0.07, P = 0.31, I² = 0), though it had only 10 studies.

**Confidence level:** High. Multiple meta-analyses converge on the same conclusion.

---

## 8. Practical Programming Differences — Summary

### Differences that ARE evidence-supported:

1. **Shorter rest periods or more volume per session.** Women's superior between-set recovery (Nuckols 2026, Hunter 2014) means they can likely use shorter rest periods (60-90s vs. 2-3 min) without the same performance decrement, or accumulate more sets in the same training time.

2. **Higher percentage of 1RM for rep work.** Because women can do more reps at a given percentage of 1RM, percentage-based programming should use slightly higher percentages for women.

3. **Proportionally more upper-body volume.** Given the larger upper-body strength/size deficit relative to lower body, women seeking balanced development may benefit from higher upper-to-lower body volume ratio.

4. **Potentially higher total weekly volume.** Women's greater fatigue resistance supports the use of higher set counts per muscle group per week, though the exact magnitude is not well-quantified.

### Differences that are NOT well-supported:

1. **Different rep ranges.** Despite fiber type differences, no strong evidence supports different rep ranges by sex.
2. **Menstrual cycle-based periodization.** Evidence too weak and methodologically flawed.
3. **Different exercise selection.** No evidence supports fundamentally different exercise choices.
4. **Different progression models.** Linear and undulating periodization work similarly for both sexes.

### Key takeaway for the Personalization Engine:

The sex layer should be **subtle**. A conservative MRV modifier of 1.05-1.10 for women, and that's largely it for the volume math. The bigger impact might be in exercise suggestions (more upper-body emphasis for women) rather than volume landmark modifications.

**Individual variation within each sex is far larger than the average between-sex difference.**
