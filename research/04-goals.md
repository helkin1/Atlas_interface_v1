# Evidence-Based Exercise Programming by Training Goal

## 1. HYPERTROPHY (Muscle Growth)

### Muscle Group Prioritization

Hypertrophy programming does not inherently prioritize one muscle group over another -- prioritization depends on the individual's aesthetic goals and weak points. However, a general framework:

- **Tier 1 (Priority):** Whichever muscles are lagging relative to the trainee's goals. Commonly: quads, back (lats/upper back), chest, glutes, deltoids -- the large muscle groups that drive the most visible change.
- **Tier 2 (Supporting):** Hamstrings, biceps, triceps, calves, rear delts.
- **Tier 3 (Maintenance):** Muscles that are already well-developed relative to the physique; forearms, neck, and other smaller groups may receive only indirect volume.

The key principle: hypertrophy programming is inherently individualized. Helms, Morgan, and Valdez in *The Muscle and Strength Pyramid: Training* (3rd ed.) recommend assessing proportional development and allocating more volume to lagging groups.

### Volume Distribution

Volume should be **concentrated on priority muscles**, not evenly distributed. The research supports:

- **Schoenfeld, Ogborn & Krieger (2017)** -- "Dose-response relationship between weekly resistance training volume and increases in muscle mass" (*Journal of Sports Sciences*, 35(11), 1073-1082): Each additional weekly set per muscle group was associated with a 0.37% increase in hypertrophy, indicating a graded dose-response.
- **Pelland et al. (2025)** -- "The Resistance Training Dose-Response: Meta-Regressions Exploring the Effects of Weekly Volume and Frequency on Muscle Hypertrophy and Strength Gain" (*Sports Medicine*): Confirmed the dose-response with a 0.24% increase per additional set at the average volume of ~12 sets/week, but with **diminishing returns** at higher volumes.
- **Baz-Valle et al. (2022)** -- Systematic review found no significant differences between moderate (~10-20 sets) and high (20+ sets) volume for quad and biceps hypertrophy, suggesting a practical ceiling.
- **Krieger (2010)** -- "Single vs. multiple sets of resistance exercise for muscle hypertrophy" (*JSCR*, 24(4), 1150-1159): Multiple sets produced ~40% greater hypertrophy effect sizes than single sets.

**Practical range:** ~10-20 sets per muscle group per week for most trainees, with priority muscles at the higher end and maintenance muscles at the lower end (~4-8 sets).

### Rep Ranges and Intensity

- **Schoenfeld et al. (2017)** -- "Strength and hypertrophy adaptations between low- vs. high-load resistance training" (*JSCR*, 31(12)): **No significant difference** in hypertrophy between low-load (<=60% 1RM) and high-load (>60% 1RM) training when taken to failure (ES difference = 0.03).
- **Schoenfeld et al. (2021)** -- "Loading Recommendations for Muscle Strength, Hypertrophy, and Local Endurance: A Re-Examination of the Repetition Continuum" (*Sports*, 9(2), 32): Hypertrophy can be achieved across a wide spectrum (approximately 30-85% 1RM), provided sets are performed with sufficient effort (close to or at failure).
- **Practical recommendation:** Most volume in the **6-30 rep range** at **30-85% 1RM**. The traditional "hypertrophy zone" of 6-12 reps at 60-80% 1RM remains practical and efficient, but is not exclusively superior.

### Frequency

- **Schoenfeld, Ogborn & Krieger (2016)** -- "Effects of Resistance Training Frequency on Measures of Muscle Hypertrophy" (*Sports Medicine*, 46(11), 1689-1697): Training each muscle **2x/week produced significantly greater hypertrophy** than 1x/week (ES 0.49 vs 0.30).
- **Schoenfeld, Grgic et al. (2019)** -- Follow-up with 25 studies: When **volume is equated**, frequency per se does not significantly affect hypertrophy. Frequency is primarily a tool for distributing volume.
- **Practical:** 2-4x per muscle group per week. Higher frequencies allow spreading volume across sessions without excessive per-session fatigue.

### Progressive Overload Strategy

- **Plotkin, Schoenfeld, Israetel et al. (2022)** -- "Progressive overload without progressing load?" (*PeerJ*): Both load progression (adding weight) and repetition progression (adding reps at same weight) produced comparable hypertrophy over 8 weeks.
- **Recommended approach:** **Double progression** -- work within a rep range (e.g., 8-12), add reps until hitting the top of the range for all sets, then increase load and drop back to the bottom of the range. Volume increases (adding sets) serve as a secondary progression tool when load/rep progression stalls.

### Exercise Selection

- **Rosa, Vazquez, Grgic et al. (2023)** -- Meta-analysis on single- vs. multi-joint exercises: No significant difference in overall hypertrophy, but isolation exercises may better target specific muscle subdivisions.
- **Haugen et al. (2023)** -- Free weights vs. machines meta-analysis (13 studies, 1016 participants): **No significant hypertrophy differences** (SMD -0.055, p=0.751).
- **Recommendation:** Use a mix of compound and isolation movements. Compounds for efficient loading of large muscle groups; isolation for targeting specific muscles and regions. Machine vs. free weight selection is a matter of preference for hypertrophy purposes.

## 2. STRENGTH / POWERLIFTING

### Muscle Group Prioritization

- **Tier 1 (Competition Movements):** The muscles directly involved in the squat, bench press, and deadlift -- quads, glutes, hamstrings, chest, anterior deltoids, triceps, spinal erectors, lats.
- **Tier 2 (Supporting/Weakness-Specific):** Upper back, rear delts, hip adductors, core stabilizers, forearms/grip. Selection depends on individual weaknesses in competition lifts.
- **Tier 3 (Maintenance/Injury Prevention):** Biceps, calves, rotator cuff, neck. Trained minimally but sufficient to prevent imbalances and injury.

Elite British powerlifters train competition lifts with high specificity: squat ~1.64x/week, bench ~2.48x/week, deadlift ~1.37x/week, using 1-7 reps at RPE 6-10 (Swinton et al., 2009 -- "Contemporary Training Practices in Elite British Powerlifters," *JSCR*).

### Volume Distribution

Volume is **heavily concentrated** on competition lifts and their close variants. Accessory work is secondary and primarily targets identified weaknesses.

- **Helms et al. (2015)** -- "Recommendations for natural bodybuilding contest preparation: resistance and cardiovascular training" (*J Sports Med Phys Fitness*): Recommends ~40-70 reps per muscle group per session, with most work in the 6-12 range at 70-80% 1RM (applicable to the hypertrophy-building phases of powerlifting).
- During peaking phases, volume decreases and specificity increases -- assistance work is cut first, and final sessions before competition are nearly exclusively the competition lifts.

### Rep Ranges and Intensity

- **Schoenfeld et al. (2017)**: High-load training (>60% 1RM) produced **significantly greater 1RM strength gains** than low-load training. This finding is driven partly by the **specificity principle** -- heavy training is more specific to the 1RM test.
- **Traditional ranges:** 1-5 reps at 80-100% 1RM for primary strength work; 6-12 reps at 65-80% 1RM for supplemental hypertrophy work.
- **Periodization:** Volume and intensity are typically inversely periodized. Accumulation phases use higher volumes at moderate intensities (65-80% 1RM); peaking phases use lower volumes at high intensities (85-100% 1RM).

### Frequency

- **Grgic, Schoenfeld et al. (2018)** -- "Effect of Resistance Training Frequency on Gains in Muscular Strength" (*Sports Medicine*): Higher training frequencies translate into **greater strength gains**, even when volume is equated.
- **Practical:** Competition lifts trained 2-4x/week. Bench press often tolerates the highest frequency; deadlift the lowest due to recovery demands.

### Progressive Overload Strategy

- **Linear periodization** for novices (adding weight each session).
- **Wave loading / block periodization** for intermediates and advanced (Helms' Muscle and Strength Pyramid recommends 3-week waves with increasing intensity and decreasing volume, followed by deloads).
- **Autoregulation via RPE/RIR:** Adjust loads session-to-session based on daily readiness. Research supports RPE-based autoregulation as equal or superior to percentage-based progression for trained lifters.

### Exercise Selection

- **Overwhelmingly compound, free-weight dominant.** The principle of specificity demands that the competition lifts and their close variants form the core of programming.
- **Heidel et al. (2021)** -- Free weights vs. machines meta-analysis: Strength gains are **modality-specific**. If the goal is barbell strength, train with barbells.
- Isolation work is supplementary, used to address weaknesses (e.g., triceps lockout weakness addressed with triceps isolation).

## 3. MUSCULAR ENDURANCE

### Muscle Group Prioritization

Prioritization depends entirely on the sport or activity:
- **Tier 1:** Muscles most used in the target endurance activity (e.g., legs for running/cycling, upper body for swimming/rowing).
- **Tier 2:** Core stabilizers, postural muscles.
- **Tier 3:** Non-sport-specific muscles -- trained for injury prevention and balance.

### Volume Distribution

Volume is more **evenly distributed** across relevant muscles than in hypertrophy or strength programs. The goal is fatigue resistance across the entire kinetic chain, not maximal development of specific muscles.

### Rep Ranges and Intensity

- **Schoenfeld et al. (2021)** -- Re-examination of the repetition continuum: The traditional recommendation of **15+ reps at <60% 1RM** for muscular endurance has support, but the evidence is "equivocal." Some studies found moderate rep ranges (15-20) superior to very high rep ranges (25+).
- High-rep training is proposed to improve **buffering capacity, capillarization, mitochondrial density**, and metabolic enzyme activity.
- **Practical:** 15-25+ reps at 30-60% 1RM, with **short rest periods** (30-90 seconds) to impose metabolic stress.

### Frequency

- 3-5 sessions per week, which can be combined with sport-specific endurance training.
- Higher frequencies are tolerable because low-load training imposes less neuromuscular fatigue.

### Progressive Overload Strategy

- Primarily through **increasing reps, reducing rest periods**, or increasing set volume, rather than load increases.
- Circuit-based progressions (increasing circuit rounds or decreasing total completion time) are also effective.

### Exercise Selection

- Mix of compound and isolation, with emphasis on movements that replicate sport demands.
- Machines can be useful for maintaining positions under fatigue.
- Bodyweight exercises, circuits, and sport-specific drills are commonly integrated.

## 4. BODY RECOMPOSITION

### Overview

- **Barakat et al. (2020)** -- "Body Recomposition: Can Trained Individuals Build Muscle and Lose Fat at the Same Time?" (*Strength & Conditioning Journal*, 42(5)): Confirmed that recomposition is **routinely observed** in research, even in trained individuals -- not just beginners. Higher baseline body fat facilitates the process; very lean individuals are less likely to gain muscle without some fat gain.

### Muscle Group Prioritization

Same as hypertrophy training -- prioritize lagging muscle groups. No special muscle group hierarchy exists for recomposition specifically.

### Volume Distribution

- **Priority muscles get more volume**, but total volume must be managed carefully due to recovery constraints in a caloric deficit.
- **Helms et al. (2014, 2015)**: During caloric restriction, maintaining (not increasing) training volume is critical for lean mass retention. If volume must be reduced, reduce it on maintenance muscles first, preserving volume on priority muscles.
- **Mitchell et al. (cited in PMC, 2022)**: Lean mass increased (+0.4 kg) when resistance training volume was maintained/increased during the first 8 weeks of a deficit, but lean mass decreased (-0.5 kg) when volume was subsequently reduced. This suggests **volume maintenance signals the body to retain muscle**.
- **Roth et al. (2023)** -- "Resistance training volume does not influence lean mass preservation during energy restriction in trained males" (*Scand J Med Sci Sports*): Challenged this somewhat, finding volume may matter less than previously assumed when protein is adequate. The debate continues.

### Rep Ranges and Intensity

- Same as hypertrophy: **6-12 reps at 60-80% 1RM** for the majority of work.
- Continue lifting heavy -- dropping intensity signals the body that maximal force production is no longer needed, potentially accelerating muscle loss.

### Frequency

- 3-5x per week. Higher frequency allows spreading volume across sessions, reducing per-session fatigue (important when recovery is compromised by the deficit).

### Progressive Overload Strategy

- **Maintaining current performance** is the primary goal during a deficit. Progression will be slower or may stall.
- Track reps and loads; if performance begins declining, consider reducing the deficit before reducing training stimulus.
- Small rep increases (rather than load increases) are more realistic during sustained deficits.

### Recomposition-Specific Considerations (Question 7)

Key factors from the literature:

1. **Protein intake:** The most critical variable. A landmark study found that 2.4 g/kg/day protein in a 40% caloric deficit allowed participants to **gain** fat-free mass, while 1.2 g/kg/day resulted in muscle loss. Helms et al. recommend **2.3-3.1 g/kg of lean body mass** during caloric restriction, with higher intakes for leaner individuals.

2. **Deficit magnitude:** Moderate deficits (~300-500 kcal/day, or ~0.5-1% bodyweight loss per week) preserve more muscle than aggressive deficits.

3. **Recovery impairment:** A caloric deficit compromises recovery capacity. This means:
   - Training volume may need to be reduced by ~1/3 compared to a surplus phase (per Helms' deload recommendations).
   - Training to failure should be limited to isolation exercises and higher-rep sets, avoiding it on heavy compound movements.
   - Sleep quality and stress management become even more critical.

4. **Rate of progress:** Recomposition is **slower** than dedicated bulk/cut cycles. It is most efficient for: beginners, individuals returning from a layoff, those with higher body fat percentages, and individuals using performance-enhancing drugs (outside the scope of this review).

## 5. GENERAL FITNESS / HEALTH

### Muscle Group Prioritization

- **Tier 1:** All major muscle groups should be trained. The emphasis is on **whole-body functional capacity**, not aesthetics. Legs (quads, glutes, hamstrings), back, and chest form the core.
- **Tier 2:** Core, shoulders, arms.
- **Tier 3:** None -- all groups should receive meaningful training for health.

The **AHA 2023 Scientific Statement** -- "Resistance Exercise Training in Individuals With and Without Cardiovascular Disease" (*Circulation*) recommends training **all major muscle groups**.

### Volume Distribution

**Even distribution** across all muscle groups. The goal is balanced development and functional capacity, not prioritization of any single group.

- Minimum effective dose: **2-4 sets per muscle group, 2x/week** appears sufficient for health benefits.
- **Shailendra et al. (2022)** -- "Resistance Training and Mortality Risk" (*American Journal of Preventive Medicine*): Maximum mortality risk reduction (~27%) was observed at **~60 minutes per week** of resistance training, with a U-shaped dose-response curve -- more is not necessarily better for health outcomes.

### Rep Ranges and Intensity

- For **bone mineral density**: Moderate to heavy loads (**>=70% 1RM**) with multi-joint free-weight exercises (squats, deadlifts) produce the most clinically meaningful improvements -- up to 3.8% BMD increase (Hong & Kim, 2018, systematic review).
- For **metabolic health and cardiovascular risk**: Moderate intensity (60-80% 1RM) with 8-15 reps appears optimal. Progressive resistance training has been shown to reduce blood pressure, improve lipid profiles, increase resting metabolic rate, and reduce cardiovascular disease risk.
- For **general populations**: 8-15 reps at moderate intensity provides a good balance of strength, hypertrophy, and metabolic benefit.

### Frequency

- **2-3 sessions per week** targeting all major muscle groups, consistent with the Physical Activity Guidelines (2nd Edition) recommendation of >=2 days of resistance training.

### Progressive Overload Strategy

- Gradual, sustainable progression. The "2-for-2 rule" is practical: increase weight when you can perform 2 extra reps beyond target for the last set, sustained for 2 consecutive sessions.
- Periodization is less critical than consistency for health outcomes.

### Exercise Selection

- Compound, multi-joint exercises are preferred for time efficiency and functional carryover.
- Free weights (especially squats and deadlifts) are superior for **bone density** outcomes due to axial loading.
- Machines are appropriate for beginners, older adults, or those with mobility limitations.

### Health-Specific Markers (Question 9)

| Health Marker | Optimal RT Parameters | Key Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **All-cause mortality** | ~60 min/week; combination RT+aerobic yields 40-46% reduction | Shailendra et al. (2022); Saeidifard et al. (2019) |
| **Bone mineral density** | >=70% 1RM, 2-3 sets, 3x/week, squats/deadlifts, >=4-6 months | Hong & Kim (2018); Frontiers meta-analysis (2023, 2025) |

## 6. CONCURRENT TRAINING: ENDURANCE + RESISTANCE (Question 8)

### The Interference Effect

- **Wilson et al. (2012)** -- "Concurrent Training: A Meta-Analysis Examining Interference of Aerobic and Resistance Exercises" (*JSCR*, 26(8), 2293-2307): The landmark meta-analysis (21 studies, 422 effect sizes) established:

| Outcome | Strength Only (ES) | Concurrent (ES) | Endurance Only (ES) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hypertrophy** | 1.23 | 0.85 | 0.27 |
| **Strength** | 1.76 | 1.44 | 0.78 |
| **Power** | 0.91 | 0.55 | 0.11 |

- Concurrent training produces **inferior** hypertrophy, strength, and power gains compared to resistance training alone, but is **still far superior** to endurance training alone for these outcomes.

### Key Moderating Variables

1. **Modality:** Running causes significantly more interference than cycling. Cycling is more compatible with resistance training because it shares a similar movement pattern (knee/hip extension) and produces less eccentric muscle damage.

2. **Frequency and duration:** Negative correlations between endurance training frequency/duration and strength/hypertrophy outcomes (r = -0.26 to -0.75). More endurance training = more interference.

3. **Practical guidelines:**
   - Separate resistance and endurance sessions by **>=6 hours** when possible.
   - Prioritize the modality trained first in the session (train the more important goal first).
   - Prefer cycling over running if minimizing interference is a priority.
   - Keep endurance volume at the **minimum effective dose** if strength/hypertrophy is the primary goal.
   - Helms et al. (2015) recommend using the **lowest frequency and duration** of cardiovascular training necessary to achieve fat loss goals during contest preparation.

Volume is **heavily concentrated** on competition lifts and their close variants. Accessory work is secondary and primarily targets identified weaknesses.

- **Helms et al. (2015)** -- "Recommendations for natural bodybuilding contest preparation: resistance and cardiovascular training" (*J Sports Med Phys Fitness*): Recommends ~40-70 reps per muscle group per session, with most work in the 6-12 range at 70-80% 1RM (applicable to the hypertrophy-building phases of powerlifting).
- During peaking phases, volume decreases and specificity increases -- assistance work is cut first, and final sessions before competition are nearly exclusively the competition lifts.

### Rep Ranges and Intensity

- **Schoenfeld et al. (2017)**: High-load training (>60% 1RM) produced **significantly greater 1RM strength gains** than low-load training. This finding is driven partly by the **specificity principle** -- heavy training is more specific to the 1RM test.
- **Traditional ranges:** 1-5 reps at 80-100% 1RM for primary strength work; 6-12 reps at 65-80% 1RM for supplemental hypertrophy work.
- **Periodization:** Volume and intensity are typically inversely periodized. Accumulation phases use higher volumes at moderate intensities (65-80% 1RM); peaking phases use lower volumes at high intensities (85-100% 1RM).

### Frequency

- **Grgic, Schoenfeld et al. (2018)** -- "Effect of Resistance Training Frequency on Gains in Muscular Strength" (*Sports Medicine*): Higher training frequencies translate into **greater strength gains**, even when volume is equated.
- **Practical:** Competition lifts trained 2-4x/week. Bench press often tolerates the highest frequency; deadlift the lowest due to recovery demands.

### Progressive Overload Strategy

- **Linear periodization** for novices (adding weight each session).
- **Wave loading / block periodization** for intermediates and advanced (Helms' Muscle and Strength Pyramid recommends 3-week waves with increasing intensity and decreasing volume, followed by deloads).
- **Autoregulation via RPE/RIR:** Adjust loads session-to-session based on daily readiness. Research supports RPE-based autoregulation as equal or superior to percentage-based progression for trained lifters.

### Exercise Selection

- **Overwhelmingly compound, free-weight dominant.** The principle of specificity demands that the competition lifts and their close variants form the core of programming.
- **Heidel et al. (2021)** -- Free weights vs. machines meta-analysis: Strength gains are **modality-specific**. If the goal is barbell strength, train with barbells.
- Isolation work is supplementary, used to address weaknesses (e.g., triceps lockout weakness addressed with triceps isolation).

## 3. MUSCULAR ENDURANCE

### Muscle Group Prioritization

Prioritization depends entirely on the sport or activity:
- **Tier 1:** Muscles most used in the target endurance activity (e.g., legs for running/cycling, upper body for swimming/rowing).
- **Tier 2:** Core stabilizers, postural muscles.
- **Tier 3:** Non-sport-specific muscles -- trained for injury prevention and balance.

### Volume Distribution

Volume is more **evenly distributed** across relevant muscles than in hypertrophy or strength programs. The goal is fatigue resistance across the entire kinetic chain, not maximal development of specific muscles.

### Rep Ranges and Intensity

- **Schoenfeld et al. (2021)** -- Re-examination of the repetition continuum: The traditional recommendation of **15+ reps at <60% 1RM** for muscular endurance has support, but the evidence is "equivocal." Some studies found moderate rep ranges (15-20) superior to very high rep ranges (25+).
- High-rep training is proposed to improve **buffering capacity, capillarization, mitochondrial density**, and metabolic enzyme activity.
- **Practical:** 15-25+ reps at 30-60% 1RM, with **short rest periods** (30-90 seconds) to impose metabolic stress.

### Frequency

- 3-5 sessions per week, which can be combined with sport-specific endurance training.
- Higher frequencies are tolerable because low-load training imposes less neuromuscular fatigue.

### Progressive Overload Strategy

- Primarily through **increasing reps, reducing rest periods**, or increasing set volume, rather than load increases.
- Circuit-based progressions (increasing circuit rounds or decreasing total completion time) are also effective.

### Exercise Selection

- Mix of compound and isolation, with emphasis on movements that replicate sport demands.
- Machines can be useful for maintaining positions under fatigue.
- Bodyweight exercises, circuits, and sport-specific drills are commonly integrated.

## 4. BODY RECOMPOSITION

### Overview

- **Barakat et al. (2020)** -- "Body Recomposition: Can Trained Individuals Build Muscle and Lose Fat at the Same Time?" (*Strength & Conditioning Journal*, 42(5)): Confirmed that recomposition is **routinely observed** in research, even in trained individuals -- not just beginners. Higher baseline body fat facilitates the process; very lean individuals are less likely to gain muscle without some fat gain.

### Muscle Group Prioritization

Same as hypertrophy training -- prioritize lagging muscle groups. No special muscle group hierarchy exists for recomposition specifically.

### Volume Distribution

- **Priority muscles get more volume**, but total volume must be managed carefully due to recovery constraints in a caloric deficit.
- **Helms et al. (2014, 2015)**: During caloric restriction, maintaining (not increasing) training volume is critical for lean mass retention. If volume must be reduced, reduce it on maintenance muscles first, preserving volume on priority muscles.
- **Mitchell et al. (cited in PMC, 2022)**: Lean mass increased (+0.4 kg) when resistance training volume was maintained/increased during the first 8 weeks of a deficit, but lean mass decreased (-0.5 kg) when volume was subsequently reduced. This suggests **volume maintenance signals the body to retain muscle**.
- **Roth et al. (2023)** -- "Resistance training volume does not influence lean mass preservation during energy restriction in trained males" (*Scand J Med Sci Sports*): Challenged this somewhat, finding volume may matter less than previously assumed when protein is adequate. The debate continues.

### Rep Ranges and Intensity

- Same as hypertrophy: **6-12 reps at 60-80% 1RM** for the majority of work.
- Continue lifting heavy -- dropping intensity signals the body that maximal force production is no longer needed, potentially accelerating muscle loss.

### Frequency

- 3-5x per week. Higher frequency allows spreading volume across sessions, reducing per-session fatigue (important when recovery is compromised by the deficit).

### Progressive Overload Strategy

- **Maintaining current performance** is the primary goal during a deficit. Progression will be slower or may stall.
- Track reps and loads; if performance begins declining, consider reducing the deficit before reducing training stimulus.
- Small rep increases (rather than load increases) are more realistic during sustained deficits.

### Recomposition-Specific Considerations (Question 7)

Key factors from the literature:

1. **Protein intake:** The most critical variable. A landmark study found that 2.4 g/kg/day protein in a 40% caloric deficit allowed participants to **gain** fat-free mass, while 1.2 g/kg/day resulted in muscle loss. Helms et al. recommend **2.3-3.1 g/kg of lean body mass** during caloric restriction, with higher intakes for leaner individuals.

2. **Deficit magnitude:** Moderate deficits (~300-500 kcal/day, or ~0.5-1% bodyweight loss per week) preserve more muscle than aggressive deficits.

3. **Recovery impairment:** A caloric deficit compromises recovery capacity. This means:
   - Training volume may need to be reduced by ~1/3 compared to a surplus phase (per Helms' deload recommendations).
   - Training to failure should be limited to isolation exercises and higher-rep sets, avoiding it on heavy compound movements.
   - Sleep quality and stress management become even more critical.

4. **Rate of progress:** Recomposition is **slower** than dedicated bulk/cut cycles. It is most efficient for: beginners, individuals returning from a layoff, those with higher body fat percentages, and individuals using performance-enhancing drugs (outside the scope of this review).

## 5. GENERAL FITNESS / HEALTH

### Muscle Group Prioritization

- **Tier 1:** All major muscle groups should be trained. The emphasis is on **whole-body functional capacity**, not aesthetics. Legs (quads, glutes, hamstrings), back, and chest form the core.
- **Tier 2:** Core, shoulders, arms.
- **Tier 3:** None -- all groups should receive meaningful training for health.

The **AHA 2023 Scientific Statement** -- "Resistance Exercise Training in Individuals With and Without Cardiovascular Disease" (*Circulation*) recommends training **all major muscle groups**.

### Volume Distribution

**Even distribution** across all muscle groups. The goal is balanced development and functional capacity, not prioritization of any single group.

- Minimum effective dose: **2-4 sets per muscle group, 2x/week** appears sufficient for health benefits.
- **Shailendra et al. (2022)** -- "Resistance Training and Mortality Risk" (*American Journal of Preventive Medicine*): Maximum mortality risk reduction (~27%) was observed at **~60 minutes per week** of resistance training, with a U-shaped dose-response curve -- more is not necessarily better for health outcomes.

### Rep Ranges and Intensity

- For **bone mineral density**: Moderate to heavy loads (**>=70% 1RM**) with multi-joint free-weight exercises (squats, deadlifts) produce the most clinically meaningful improvements -- up to 3.8% BMD increase (Hong & Kim, 2018, systematic review).
- For **metabolic health and cardiovascular risk**: Moderate intensity (60-80% 1RM) with 8-15 reps appears optimal. Progressive resistance training has been shown to reduce blood pressure, improve lipid profiles, increase resting metabolic rate, and reduce cardiovascular disease risk.
- For **general populations**: 8-15 reps at moderate intensity provides a good balance of strength, hypertrophy, and metabolic benefit.

### Frequency

- **2-3 sessions per week** targeting all major muscle groups, consistent with the Physical Activity Guidelines (2nd Edition) recommendation of >=2 days of resistance training.

### Progressive Overload Strategy

- Gradual, sustainable progression. The "2-for-2 rule" is practical: increase weight when you can perform 2 extra reps beyond target for the last set, sustained for 2 consecutive sessions.
- Periodization is less critical than consistency for health outcomes.

### Exercise Selection

- Compound, multi-joint exercises are preferred for time efficiency and functional carryover.
- Free weights (especially squats and deadlifts) are superior for **bone density** outcomes due to axial loading.
- Machines are appropriate for beginners, older adults, or those with mobility limitations.

## 6. CONCURRENT TRAINING: ENDURANCE + RESISTANCE (Question 8)

### The Interference Effect

- **Wilson et al. (2012)** -- "Concurrent Training: A Meta-Analysis Examining Interference of Aerobic and Resistance Exercises" (*JSCR*, 26(8), 2293-2307): The landmark meta-analysis (21 studies, 422 effect sizes) established:

| Outcome | Strength Only (ES) | Concurrent (ES) | Endurance Only (ES) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hypertrophy** | 1.23 | 0.85 | 0.27 |
| **Strength** | 1.76 | 1.44 | 0.78 |
| **Power** | 0.91 | 0.55 | 0.11 |

- Concurrent training produces **inferior** hypertrophy, strength, and power gains compared to resistance training alone, but is **still far superior** to endurance training alone for these outcomes.

### Key Moderating Variables

1. **Modality:** Running causes significantly more interference than cycling. Cycling is more compatible with resistance training because it shares a similar movement pattern (knee/hip extension) and produces less eccentric muscle damage.

2. **Frequency and duration:** Negative correlations between endurance training frequency/duration and strength/hypertrophy outcomes (r = -0.26 to -0.75). More endurance training = more interference.

3. **Practical guidelines:**
   - Separate resistance and endurance sessions by **>=6 hours** when possible.
   - Prioritize the modality trained first in the session (train the more important goal first).
   - Prefer cycling over running if minimizing interference is a priority.
   - Keep endurance volume at the **minimum effective dose** if strength/hypertrophy is the primary goal.
   - Helms et al. (2015) recommend using the **lowest frequency and duration** of cardiovascular training necessary to achieve fat loss goals during contest preparation.

# Evidence-Based Exercise Programming by Training Goal

## 1. HYPERTROPHY (Muscle Growth)

### Muscle Group Prioritization

Hypertrophy programming does not inherently prioritize one muscle group over another -- prioritization depends on the individual's aesthetic goals and weak points. However, a general framework:

- **Tier 1 (Priority):** Whichever muscles are lagging relative to the trainee's goals. Commonly: quads, back (lats/upper back), chest, glutes, deltoids -- the large muscle groups that drive the most visible change.
- **Tier 2 (Supporting):** Hamstrings, biceps, triceps, calves, rear delts.
- **Tier 3 (Maintenance):** Muscles that are already well-developed relative to the physique; forearms, neck, and other smaller groups may receive only indirect volume.

The key principle: hypertrophy programming is inherently individualized. Helms, Morgan, and Valdez in *The Muscle and Strength Pyramid: Training* (3rd ed.) recommend assessing proportional development and allocating more volume to lagging groups.

### Volume Distribution

Volume should be **concentrated on priority muscles**, not evenly distributed. The research supports:

- **Schoenfeld, Ogborn & Krieger (2017)** -- "Dose-response relationship between weekly resistance training volume and increases in muscle mass" (*Journal of Sports Sciences*, 35(11), 1073-1082): Each additional weekly set per muscle group was associated with a 0.37% increase in hypertrophy, indicating a graded dose-response.
- **Pelland et al. (2025)** -- "The Resistance Training Dose-Response: Meta-Regressions Exploring the Effects of Weekly Volume and Frequency on Muscle Hypertrophy and Strength Gain" (*Sports Medicine*): Confirmed the dose-response with a 0.24% increase per additional set at the average volume of ~12 sets/week, but with **diminishing returns** at higher volumes.
- **Baz-Valle et al. (2022)** -- Systematic review found no significant differences between moderate (~10-20 sets) and high (20+ sets) volume for quad and biceps hypertrophy, suggesting a practical ceiling.
- **Krieger (2010)** -- "Single vs. multiple sets of resistance exercise for muscle hypertrophy" (*JSCR*, 24(4), 1150-1159): Multiple sets produced ~40% greater hypertrophy effect sizes than single sets.

**Practical range:** ~10-20 sets per muscle group per week for most trainees, with priority muscles at the higher end and maintenance muscles at the lower end (~4-8 sets).

### Rep Ranges and Intensity

- **Schoenfeld et al. (2017)** -- "Strength and hypertrophy adaptations between low- vs. high-load resistance training" (*JSCR*, 31(12)): **No significant difference** in hypertrophy between low-load (<=60% 1RM) and high-load (>60% 1RM) training when taken to failure (ES difference = 0.03).
- **Schoenfeld et al. (2021)** -- "Loading Recommendations for Muscle Strength, Hypertrophy, and Local Endurance: A Re-Examination of the Repetition Continuum" (*Sports*, 9(2), 32): Hypertrophy can be achieved across a wide spectrum (approximately 30-85% 1RM), provided sets are performed with sufficient effort (close to or at failure).
- **Practical recommendation:** Most volume in the **6-30 rep range** at **30-85% 1RM**. The traditional "hypertrophy zone" of 6-12 reps at 60-80% 1RM remains practical and efficient, but is not exclusively superior.

### Frequency

- **Schoenfeld, Ogborn & Krieger (2016)** -- "Effects of Resistance Training Frequency on Measures of Muscle Hypertrophy" (*Sports Medicine*, 46(11), 1689-1697): Training each muscle **2x/week produced significantly greater hypertrophy** than 1x/week (ES 0.49 vs 0.30).
- **Schoenfeld, Grgic et al. (2019)** -- Follow-up with 25 studies: When **volume is equated**, frequency per se does not significantly affect hypertrophy. Frequency is primarily a tool for distributing volume.
- **Practical:** 2-4x per muscle group per week. Higher frequencies allow spreading volume across sessions without excessive per-session fatigue.

### Progressive Overload Strategy

- **Plotkin, Schoenfeld, Israetel et al. (2022)** -- "Progressive overload without progressing load?" (*PeerJ*): Both load progression (adding weight) and repetition progression (adding reps at same weight) produced comparable hypertrophy over 8 weeks.
- **Recommended approach:** **Double progression** -- work within a rep range (e.g., 8-12), add reps until hitting the top of the range for all sets, then increase load and drop back to the bottom of the range. Volume increases (adding sets) serve as a secondary progression tool when load/rep progression stalls.

### Exercise Selection

- **Rosa, Vazquez, Grgic et al. (2023)** -- Meta-analysis on single- vs. multi-joint exercises: No significant difference in overall hypertrophy, but isolation exercises may better target specific muscle subdivisions.
- **Haugen et al. (2023)** -- Free weights vs. machines meta-analysis (13 studies, 1016 participants): **No significant hypertrophy differences** (SMD -0.055, p=0.751).
- **Recommendation:** Use a mix of compound and isolation movements. Compounds for efficient loading of large muscle groups; isolation for targeting specific muscles and regions. Machine vs. free weight selection is a matter of preference for hypertrophy purposes.

- Same as hypertrophy: **6-12 reps at 60-80% 1RM** for the majority of work.
- Continue lifting heavy -- dropping intensity signals the body that maximal force production is no longer needed, potentially accelerating muscle loss.

### Frequency

- 3-5x per week. Higher frequency allows spreading volume across sessions, reducing per-session fatigue (important when recovery is compromised by the deficit).

### Progressive Overload Strategy

- **Maintaining current performance** is the primary goal during a deficit. Progression will be slower or may stall.
- Track reps and loads; if performance begins declining, consider reducing the deficit before reducing training stimulus.
- Small rep increases (rather than load increases) are more realistic during sustained deficits.

### Recomposition-Specific Considerations (Question 7)

Key factors from the literature:

1. **Protein intake:** The most critical variable. A landmark study found that 2.4 g/kg/day protein in a 40% caloric deficit allowed participants to **gain** fat-free mass, while 1.2 g/kg/day resulted in muscle loss. Helms et al. recommend **2.3-3.1 g/kg of lean body mass** during caloric restriction, with higher intakes for leaner individuals.

2. **Deficit magnitude:** Moderate deficits (~300-500 kcal/day, or ~0.5-1% bodyweight loss per week) preserve more muscle than aggressive deficits.

3. **Recovery impairment:** A caloric deficit compromises recovery capacity. This means:
   - Training volume may need to be reduced by ~1/3 compared to a surplus phase (per Helms' deload recommendations).
   - Training to failure should be limited to isolation exercises and higher-rep sets, avoiding it on heavy compound movements.
   - Sleep quality and stress management become even more critical.

4. **Rate of progress:** Recomposition is **slower** than dedicated bulk/cut cycles. It is most efficient for: beginners, individuals returning from a layoff, those with higher body fat percentages, and individuals using performance-enhancing drugs (outside the scope of this review).

## 5. GENERAL FITNESS / HEALTH

### Muscle Group Prioritization

- **Tier 1:** All major muscle groups should be trained. The emphasis is on **whole-body functional capacity**, not aesthetics. Legs (quads, glutes, hamstrings), back, and chest form the core.
- **Tier 2:** Core, shoulders, arms.
- **Tier 3:** None -- all groups should receive meaningful training for health.

The **AHA 2023 Scientific Statement** -- "Resistance Exercise Training in Individuals With and Without Cardiovascular Disease" (*Circulation*) recommends training **all major muscle groups**.

### Volume Distribution

**Even distribution** across all muscle groups. The goal is balanced development and functional capacity, not prioritization of any single group.

- Minimum effective dose: **2-4 sets per muscle group, 2x/week** appears sufficient for health benefits.
- **Shailendra et al. (2022)** -- "Resistance Training and Mortality Risk" (*American Journal of Preventive Medicine*): Maximum mortality risk reduction (~27%) was observed at **~60 minutes per week** of resistance training, with a U-shaped dose-response curve -- more is not necessarily better for health outcomes.

### Rep Ranges and Intensity

- For **bone mineral density**: Moderate to heavy loads (**>=70% 1RM**) with multi-joint free-weight exercises (squats, deadlifts) produce the most clinically meaningful improvements -- up to 3.8% BMD increase (Hong & Kim, 2018, systematic review).
- For **metabolic health and cardiovascular risk**: Moderate intensity (60-80% 1RM) with 8-15 reps appears optimal. Progressive resistance training has been shown to reduce blood pressure, improve lipid profiles, increase resting metabolic rate, and reduce cardiovascular disease risk.
- For **general populations**: 8-15 reps at moderate intensity provides a good balance of strength, hypertrophy, and metabolic benefit.

### Frequency

- **2-3 sessions per week** targeting all major muscle groups, consistent with the Physical Activity Guidelines (2nd Edition) recommendation of >=2 days of resistance training.

### Progressive Overload Strategy

- Gradual, sustainable progression. The "2-for-2 rule" is practical: increase weight when you can perform 2 extra reps beyond target for the last set, sustained for 2 consecutive sessions.
- Periodization is less critical than consistency for health outcomes.

### Exercise Selection

- Compound, multi-joint exercises are preferred for time efficiency and functional carryover.
- Free weights (especially squats and deadlifts) are superior for **bone density** outcomes due to axial loading.
- Machines are appropriate for beginners, older adults, or those with mobility limitations.

|---|---|---|
| **All-cause mortality** | ~60 min/week; combination RT+aerobic yields 40-46% reduction | Shailendra et al. (2022); Saeidifard et al. (2019) |

## 6. CONCURRENT TRAINING: ENDURANCE + RESISTANCE (Question 8)

### The Interference Effect

- **Wilson et al. (2012)** -- "Concurrent Training: A Meta-Analysis Examining Interference of Aerobic and Resistance Exercises" (*JSCR*, 26(8), 2293-2307): The landmark meta-analysis (21 studies, 422 effect sizes) established:

| Outcome | Strength Only (ES) | Concurrent (ES) | Endurance Only (ES) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hypertrophy** | 1.23 | 0.85 | 0.27 |
| **Strength** | 1.76 | 1.44 | 0.78 |
| **Power** | 0.91 | 0.55 | 0.11 |

- Concurrent training produces **inferior** hypertrophy, strength, and power gains compared to resistance training alone, but is **still far superior** to endurance training alone for these outcomes.

### Key Moderating Variables

1. **Modality:** Running causes significantly more interference than cycling. Cycling is more compatible with resistance training because it shares a similar movement pattern (knee/hip extension) and produces less eccentric muscle damage.

2. **Frequency and duration:** Negative correlations between endurance training frequency/duration and strength/hypertrophy outcomes (r = -0.26 to -0.75). More endurance training = more interference.

3. **Practical guidelines:**
   - Separate resistance and endurance sessions by **>=6 hours** when possible.
   - Prioritize the modality trained first in the session (train the more important goal first).
   - Prefer cycling over running if minimizing interference is a priority.
   - Keep endurance volume at the **minimum effective dose** if strength/hypertrophy is the primary goal.
   - Helms et al. (2015) recommend using the **lowest frequency and duration** of cardiovascular training necessary to achieve fat loss goals during contest preparation.

# Evidence-Based Exercise Programming by Training Goal

## 1. HYPERTROPHY (Muscle Growth)

### Muscle Group Prioritization

Hypertrophy programming does not inherently prioritize one muscle group over another -- prioritization depends on the individual's aesthetic goals and weak points. However, a general framework:

- **Tier 1 (Priority):** Whichever muscles are lagging relative to the trainee's goals. Commonly: quads, back (lats/upper back), chest, glutes, deltoids -- the large muscle groups that drive the most visible change.
- **Tier 2 (Supporting):** Hamstrings, biceps, triceps, calves, rear delts.
- **Tier 3 (Maintenance):** Muscles that are already well-developed relative to the physique; forearms, neck, and other smaller groups may receive only indirect volume.

The key principle: hypertrophy programming is inherently individualized. Helms, Morgan, and Valdez in *The Muscle and Strength Pyramid: Training* (3rd ed.) recommend assessing proportional development and allocating more volume to lagging groups.

### Volume Distribution

Volume should be **concentrated on priority muscles**, not evenly distributed. The research supports:

- **Schoenfeld, Ogborn & Krieger (2017)** -- "Dose-response relationship between weekly resistance training volume and increases in muscle mass" (*Journal of Sports Sciences*, 35(11), 1073-1082): Each additional weekly set per muscle group was associated with a 0.37% increase in hypertrophy, indicating a graded dose-response.
- **Pelland et al. (2025)** -- "The Resistance Training Dose-Response: Meta-Regressions Exploring the Effects of Weekly Volume and Frequency on Muscle Hypertrophy and Strength Gain" (*Sports Medicine*): Confirmed the dose-response with a 0.24% increase per additional set at the average volume of ~12 sets/week, but with **diminishing returns** at higher volumes.
- **Baz-Valle et al. (2022)** -- Systematic review found no significant differences between moderate (~10-20 sets) and high (20+ sets) volume for quad and biceps hypertrophy, suggesting a practical ceiling.
- **Krieger (2010)** -- "Single vs. multiple sets of resistance exercise for muscle hypertrophy" (*JSCR*, 24(4), 1150-1159): Multiple sets produced ~40% greater hypertrophy effect sizes than single sets.

**Practical range:** ~10-20 sets per muscle group per week for most trainees, with priority muscles at the higher end and maintenance muscles at the lower end (~4-8 sets).

### Rep Ranges and Intensity

- **Schoenfeld et al. (2017)** -- "Strength and hypertrophy adaptations between low- vs. high-load resistance training" (*JSCR*, 31(12)): **No significant difference** in hypertrophy between low-load (<=60% 1RM) and high-load (>60% 1RM) training when taken to failure (ES difference = 0.03).
- **Schoenfeld et al. (2021)** -- "Loading Recommendations for Muscle Strength, Hypertrophy, and Local Endurance: A Re-Examination of the Repetition Continuum" (*Sports*, 9(2), 32): Hypertrophy can be achieved across a wide spectrum (approximately 30-85% 1RM), provided sets are performed with sufficient effort (close to or at failure).
- **Practical recommendation:** Most volume in the **6-30 rep range** at **30-85% 1RM**. The traditional "hypertrophy zone" of 6-12 reps at 60-80% 1RM remains practical and efficient, but is not exclusively superior.

### Frequency

- **Schoenfeld, Ogborn & Krieger (2016)** -- "Effects of Resistance Training Frequency on Measures of Muscle Hypertrophy" (*Sports Medicine*, 46(11), 1689-1697): Training each muscle **2x/week produced significantly greater hypertrophy** than 1x/week (ES 0.49 vs 0.30).
- **Schoenfeld, Grgic et al. (2019)** -- Follow-up with 25 studies: When **volume is equated**, frequency per se does not significantly affect hypertrophy. Frequency is primarily a tool for distributing volume.
- **Practical:** 2-4x per muscle group per week. Higher frequencies allow spreading volume across sessions without excessive per-session fatigue.

### Progressive Overload Strategy

- **Plotkin, Schoenfeld, Israetel et al. (2022)** -- "Progressive overload without progressing load?" (*PeerJ*): Both load progression (adding weight) and repetition progression (adding reps at same weight) produced comparable hypertrophy over 8 weeks.
- **Recommended approach:** **Double progression** -- work within a rep range (e.g., 8-12), add reps until hitting the top of the range for all sets, then increase load and drop back to the bottom of the range. Volume increases (adding sets) serve as a secondary progression tool when load/rep progression stalls.

### Exercise Selection

- **Rosa, Vazquez, Grgic et al. (2023)** -- Meta-analysis on single- vs. multi-joint exercises: No significant difference in overall hypertrophy, but isolation exercises may better target specific muscle subdivisions.
- **Haugen et al. (2023)** -- Free weights vs. machines meta-analysis (13 studies, 1016 participants): **No significant hypertrophy differences** (SMD -0.055, p=0.751).
- **Recommendation:** Use a mix of compound and isolation movements. Compounds for efficient loading of large muscle groups; isolation for targeting specific muscles and regions. Machine vs. free weight selection is a matter of preference for hypertrophy purposes.

## 2. STRENGTH / POWERLIFTING

### Muscle Group Prioritization

- **Tier 1 (Competition Movements):** The muscles directly involved in the squat, bench press, and deadlift -- quads, glutes, hamstrings, chest, anterior deltoids, triceps, spinal erectors, lats.
- **Tier 2 (Supporting/Weakness-Specific):** Upper back, rear delts, hip adductors, core stabilizers, forearms/grip. Selection depends on individual weaknesses in competition lifts.
- **Tier 3 (Maintenance/Injury Prevention):** Biceps, calves, rotator cuff, neck. Trained minimally but sufficient to prevent imbalances and injury.

Elite British powerlifters train competition lifts with high specificity: squat ~1.64x/week, bench ~2.48x/week, deadlift ~1.37x/week, using 1-7 reps at RPE 6-10 (Swinton et al., 2009 -- "Contemporary Training Practices in Elite British Powerlifters," *JSCR*).

### Volume Distribution

Volume is **heavily concentrated** on competition lifts and their close variants. Accessory work is secondary and primarily targets identified weaknesses.

- **Helms et al. (2015)** -- "Recommendations for natural bodybuilding contest preparation: resistance and cardiovascular training" (*J Sports Med Phys Fitness*): Recommends ~40-70 reps per muscle group per session, with most work in the 6-12 range at 70-80% 1RM (applicable to the hypertrophy-building phases of powerlifting).
- During peaking phases, volume decreases and specificity increases -- assistance work is cut first, and final sessions before competition are nearly exclusively the competition lifts.

### Rep Ranges and Intensity

- **Schoenfeld et al. (2017)**: High-load training (>60% 1RM) produced **significantly greater 1RM strength gains** than low-load training. This finding is driven partly by the **specificity principle** -- heavy training is more specific to the 1RM test.
- **Traditional ranges:** 1-5 reps at 80-100% 1RM for primary strength work; 6-12 reps at 65-80% 1RM for supplemental hypertrophy work.
- **Periodization:** Volume and intensity are typically inversely periodized. Accumulation phases use higher volumes at moderate intensities (65-80% 1RM); peaking phases use lower volumes at high intensities (85-100% 1RM).

### Frequency

- **Grgic, Schoenfeld et al. (2018)** -- "Effect of Resistance Training Frequency on Gains in Muscular Strength" (*Sports Medicine*): Higher training frequencies translate into **greater strength gains**, even when volume is equated.
- **Practical:** Competition lifts trained 2-4x/week. Bench press often tolerates the highest frequency; deadlift the lowest due to recovery demands.

### Progressive Overload Strategy

- **Linear periodization** for novices (adding weight each session).
- **Wave loading / block periodization** for intermediates and advanced (Helms' Muscle and Strength Pyramid recommends 3-week waves with increasing intensity and decreasing volume, followed by deloads).
- **Autoregulation via RPE/RIR:** Adjust loads session-to-session based on daily readiness. Research supports RPE-based autoregulation as equal or superior to percentage-based progression for trained lifters.

### Exercise Selection

- **Overwhelmingly compound, free-weight dominant.** The principle of specificity demands that the competition lifts and their close variants form the core of programming.
- **Heidel et al. (2021)** -- Free weights vs. machines meta-analysis: Strength gains are **modality-specific**. If the goal is barbell strength, train with barbells.
- Isolation work is supplementary, used to address weaknesses (e.g., triceps lockout weakness addressed with triceps isolation).

## 3. MUSCULAR ENDURANCE

### Muscle Group Prioritization

Prioritization depends entirely on the sport or activity:
- **Tier 1:** Muscles most used in the target endurance activity (e.g., legs for running/cycling, upper body for swimming/rowing).
- **Tier 2:** Core stabilizers, postural muscles.
- **Tier 3:** Non-sport-specific muscles -- trained for injury prevention and balance.

### Volume Distribution

Volume is more **evenly distributed** across relevant muscles than in hypertrophy or strength programs. The goal is fatigue resistance across the entire kinetic chain, not maximal development of specific muscles.

### Rep Ranges and Intensity

- **Schoenfeld et al. (2021)** -- Re-examination of the repetition continuum: The traditional recommendation of **15+ reps at <60% 1RM** for muscular endurance has support, but the evidence is "equivocal." Some studies found moderate rep ranges (15-20) superior to very high rep ranges (25+).
- High-rep training is proposed to improve **buffering capacity, capillarization, mitochondrial density**, and metabolic enzyme activity.
- **Practical:** 15-25+ reps at 30-60% 1RM, with **short rest periods** (30-90 seconds) to impose metabolic stress.

### Frequency

- 3-5 sessions per week, which can be combined with sport-specific endurance training.
- Higher frequencies are tolerable because low-load training imposes less neuromuscular fatigue.

### Progressive Overload Strategy

- Primarily through **increasing reps, reducing rest periods**, or increasing set volume, rather than load increases.
- Circuit-based progressions (increasing circuit rounds or decreasing total completion time) are also effective.

### Exercise Selection

- Mix of compound and isolation, with emphasis on movements that replicate sport demands.
- Machines can be useful for maintaining positions under fatigue.
- Bodyweight exercises, circuits, and sport-specific drills are commonly integrated.

## 4. BODY RECOMPOSITION

### Overview

- **Barakat et al. (2020)** -- "Body Recomposition: Can Trained Individuals Build Muscle and Lose Fat at the Same Time?" (*Strength & Conditioning Journal*, 42(5)): Confirmed that recomposition is **routinely observed** in research, even in trained individuals -- not just beginners. Higher baseline body fat facilitates the process; very lean individuals are less likely to gain muscle without some fat gain.

### Muscle Group Prioritization

Same as hypertrophy training -- prioritize lagging muscle groups. No special muscle group hierarchy exists for recomposition specifically.

### Volume Distribution

- **Priority muscles get more volume**, but total volume must be managed carefully due to recovery constraints in a caloric deficit.
- **Helms et al. (2014, 2015)**: During caloric restriction, maintaining (not increasing) training volume is critical for lean mass retention. If volume must be reduced, reduce it on maintenance muscles first, preserving volume on priority muscles.
- **Mitchell et al. (cited in PMC, 2022)**: Lean mass increased (+0.4 kg) when resistance training volume was maintained/increased during the first 8 weeks of a deficit, but lean mass decreased (-0.5 kg) when volume was subsequently reduced. This suggests **volume maintenance signals the body to retain muscle**.
- **Roth et al. (2023)** -- "Resistance training volume does not influence lean mass preservation during energy restriction in trained males" (*Scand J Med Sci Sports*): Challenged this somewhat, finding volume may matter less than previously assumed when protein is adequate. The debate continues.

### Rep Ranges and Intensity

- Same as hypertrophy: **6-12 reps at 60-80% 1RM** for the majority of work.
- Continue lifting heavy -- dropping intensity signals the body that maximal force production is no longer needed, potentially accelerating muscle loss.

### Frequency

- 3-5x per week. Higher frequency allows spreading volume across sessions, reducing per-session fatigue (important when recovery is compromised by the deficit).

### Progressive Overload Strategy

- **Maintaining current performance** is the primary goal during a deficit. Progression will be slower or may stall.
- Track reps and loads; if performance begins declining, consider reducing the deficit before reducing training stimulus.
- Small rep increases (rather than load increases) are more realistic during sustained deficits.

### Recomposition-Specific Considerations (Question 7)

Key factors from the literature:

1. **Protein intake:** The most critical variable. A landmark study found that 2.4 g/kg/day protein in a 40% caloric deficit allowed participants to **gain** fat-free mass, while 1.2 g/kg/day resulted in muscle loss. Helms et al. recommend **2.3-3.1 g/kg of lean body mass** during caloric restriction, with higher intakes for leaner individuals.

2. **Deficit magnitude:** Moderate deficits (~300-500 kcal/day, or ~0.5-1% bodyweight loss per week) preserve more muscle than aggressive deficits.

3. **Recovery impairment:** A caloric deficit compromises recovery capacity. This means:
   - Training volume may need to be reduced by ~1/3 compared to a surplus phase (per Helms' recommendations).
   - Training to failure should be limited to isolation exercises and higher-rep sets, avoiding it on heavy compound movements.
   - Sleep quality and stress management become even more critical.

4. **Rate of progress:** Recomposition is **slower** than dedicated bulk/cut cycles. It is most efficient for: beginners, individuals returning from a layoff, those with higher body fat percentages, and individuals using performance-enhancing drugs (outside the scope of this review).

|---|---|---|---|
| **Hypertrophy** | 1.23 | 0.85 | 0.27 |
| **Strength** | 1.76 | 1.44 | 0.78 |
| **Power** | 0.91 | 0.55 | 0.11 |

- Concurrent training produces **inferior** hypertrophy, strength, and power gains compared to resistance training alone, but is **still far superior** to endurance training alone for these outcomes.

### Key Moderating Variables

1. **Modality:** Running causes significantly more interference than cycling. Cycling is more compatible with resistance training because it shares a similar movement pattern (knee/hip extension) and produces less eccentric muscle damage.

2. **Frequency and duration:** Negative correlations between endurance training frequency/duration and strength/hypertrophy outcomes (r = -0.26 to -0.75). More endurance training = more interference.

3. **Practical guidelines:**
   - Separate resistance and endurance sessions by **>=6 hours** when possible.
   - Prioritize the modality trained first in the session (train the more important goal first).
   - Prefer cycling over running if minimizing interference is a priority.
   - Keep endurance volume at the **minimum effective dose** if strength/hypertrophy is the primary goal.
   - Helms et al. (2015) recommend using the **lowest frequency and duration** of cardiovascular training necessary to achieve fat loss goals during contest preparation.
